Planning a wedding involves managing dozens of individual contracts. Unlike hiring a contractor for a home renovation, where you might see the progress daily, wedding vendors are often "booked and forgotten" until the big day. Because of this, the vetting process needs to happen entirely in the first few weeks of contact. 

I learned the hard way that a beautiful Instagram feed doesn’t guarantee a professional experience. Here are seven specific red flags I look for now, and that you should watch for before you put down a non-refundable deposit.

## 1. The "Ghosting" Phase During Inquiry
If a vendor takes more than three or four business days to respond to your initial inquiry, pay attention. This isn't just a sign of a busy professional; it is a preview of how they will communicate when you have a genuine crisis three weeks before your wedding.

If their initial sales pitch is slow, disorganized, or comes from a generic "info@" email address with no signature, walk away. You want a vendor who treats your first contact with the same urgency as they will on the day of the event. If they can’t manage a simple calendar invite during the booking phase, they aren’t going to magically become organized on your wedding day.

## 2. Vague or Missing Contract Clauses
Never accept a "standard" contract without reading the liability and cancellation sections. If a vendor’s contract doesn't explicitly state what happens if they fall ill, have a car accident, or suffer a family emergency, that is a massive red flag.

You are looking for a "force majeure" or "substitution" clause. A professional vendor will have a backup plan—usually a network of peers they can call to cover for them. If the contract says, "In the event of cancellation by the vendor, all monies paid are non-refundable," without offering a replacement, they are essentially telling you that your wedding is a financial risk they aren't willing to share.

## 3. Pushing You to Use Their "Preferred" Vendors
Many venues have a "preferred vendor list." While some of these are based on quality, many are based on kickbacks—vendors pay the venue a fee to be on that list. 

If a venue manager or a planner tells you that you *must* use their specific DJ or florist, or else you have to pay a "surcharge" for outside vendors, ask for the breakdown of that fee. If they refuse to allow you to bring in outside talent, or they badmouth every vendor not on their list, they are likely protecting a commission stream rather than your best interests. A good vendor should be confident enough in their work to stand alongside anyone else you choose.

## 4. Lack of Recent, Consistent Portfolios
When looking at a photographer, videographer, or florist, ignore their "best of" highlight reel. Everyone can get five lucky shots over the course of three years. Instead, ask to see a full, unedited gallery from a wedding they did within the last six months.

If they refuse to share a full gallery, or if the lighting, editing, and style vary wildly from one event to the next, it means they don't have a consistent workflow. You need to know that the person you hire can handle a dark, rainy reception just as well as they can a bright, sunny ceremony.

## 5. They Are "Yes" People
This sounds counterintuitive, but be wary of a vendor who agrees to everything without question. If you ask a florist for out-of-season peonies in the middle of winter, or a caterer for a complex plated dinner for 200 people with only two servers, a professional will tell you the risks.

A vendor who says, "Sure, we can do that!" to every impossible request is either setting you up for a massive upcharge later or doesn't know their own operational limits. You want someone who acts as a consultant. If they don't push back on your unrealistic ideas, they don't have the experience to keep your day on schedule.

## 6. Payment Methods That Lack Protection
Avoid any vendor who insists on cash, Venmo (personal account), or wire transfers as the *only* way to pay. Using a credit card or a business-based payment processor provides you with a paper trail and potential dispute resolution if the vendor completely fails to show up. 

If they are avoiding credit cards to save on processing fees, that’s one thing. If they are avoiding them to avoid a trail of income or to prevent you from being able to issue a chargeback, that is a warning sign. Always pay at least the deposit via a method that is traceable and protected.

## 7. They Don't Have a Business Insurance Policy
This is the most boring, yet most important, question you can ask. "Do you carry general liability insurance, and can you provide a Certificate of Insurance (COI)?" 

Many venues require this. If a vendor says they don’t carry insurance, or they seem confused by the question, it means they are essentially a hobbyist. If they trip over a cord and break their equipment, or if they accidentally damage the venue’s floor, you could be the one held liable. If they don't have insurance, they aren't a business—they’re an amateur with a website.
