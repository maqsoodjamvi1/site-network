When I started my first side hustle—freelance graphic design—I treated my bank account like a black hole. Money came in from clients, and money went out for software subscriptions and coffee shop snacks. When tax season arrived, I had no idea what was a "business expense" and what was just my personal spending. I ended up leaving hundreds of dollars on the table because I didn't keep receipts for the right things.

If you are earning extra income on the side, the IRS considers you a sole proprietor. This means you have to pay self-employment tax on your net profit. The secret to keeping more of that money is understanding what counts as an "ordinary and necessary" expense. If you spend money to help your business earn revenue, you can usually subtract that from your total income, which lowers the amount you get taxed on.

## The "Ordinary and Necessary" Rule
The IRS defines a deductible expense as one that is "ordinary" (common and accepted in your trade) and "necessary" (helpful and appropriate for your trade).

If you are a freelance writer, a high-end laptop is necessary. If you are a dog walker, a pair of durable, comfortable sneakers is necessary. However, if you are a freelance writer and you buy a tuxedo for a gala you’re attending, that’s not deductible. It’s personal clothing. The golden rule is: If you can’t explain to an auditor how this specific purchase directly helped you earn income for your side hustle, don’t try to deduct it.

## Common Deductible Categories
You don't need a fancy accounting degree to categorize your expenses. Most side hustlers fall into these specific buckets:

*   **Software and Subscriptions:** Adobe Creative Cloud, Canva Pro, Microsoft 365, or specialized project management tools like Asana or Trello. If you pay a monthly fee to run your business, it’s deductible.
*   **Marketing and Advertising:** Money spent on Facebook or Instagram ads to promote your services, business cards, or even the fee you paid to set up a professional website domain.
*   **Supplies and Equipment:** This includes pens, paper, shipping labels, or larger items like cameras, microphones, or printers. Note: Items that last longer than a year—like a $2,000 laptop—are technically "assets" that you may need to depreciate over several years, rather than deducting the full cost at once.
*   **Professional Services:** Fees paid to a lawyer for a contract template, a tax professional to file your return, or an online course that directly improves your specific business skills.
*   **Business Travel:** If you have to drive to a client meeting or to a supply store, you can deduct your mileage. You cannot deduct your daily commute to your 9-to-5 job, but trips specifically related to your side hustle are fair game.

## Stop Mixing Your Money
The biggest mistake beginners make is using their personal checking account for business expenses. If your Netflix subscription and your professional liability insurance payment are coming out of the same account, you are creating a nightmare for yourself.

Open a separate, free checking account for your side hustle. Link your income (from platforms like Upwork, Etsy, or Venmo) to this account, and use its debit card exclusively for business purchases. At the end of the year, you can simply download your statement, and every transaction on that list is either business income or a potential deduction.

## Mileage Tracking: Don’t Guess
If you use your car for work—like delivering for DoorDash or driving to pick up inventory for your eBay shop—you should track your miles. You cannot deduct both gas *and* standard mileage; you have to choose one. Most people find the standard mileage rate (set annually by the IRS) much easier to manage.

Don't wait until December to estimate your miles. Use an app like MileIQ or even a simple physical notebook in your glove box. Write down the date, the destination, the business purpose, and the odometer reading. If you get audited, a "guesstimate" of 5,000 miles won't hold up, but a logbook will.

## What You Cannot Deduct
It is just as important to know what the IRS will flag. Do not try to write off your home internet bill unless you can prove that you pay for a separate, dedicated business line. You cannot deduct the cost of clothes you wear every day (like jeans and t-shirts), even if you wear them while working. You also cannot deduct "startup costs" for a business that never officially launched or earned any income.

Keep your documentation for at least three years. If you buy something online, print the receipt as a PDF and save it in a folder labeled by tax year. If you buy something in person, take a photo of the receipt; thermal paper fades over time, and a physical receipt might be unreadable by the time you actually need it for your taxes.

Tax tracking doesn't have to be a source of anxiety. Once you separate your accounts and get into the habit of logging expenses as they happen, it becomes a simple monthly routine rather than a frantic end-of-year scramble. Mastering these basics now will set a professional foundation for your hustle as it grows.

Now that you have a better grasp of how to categorize your expenses and maintain the necessary documentation, your next step is to evaluate your current setup. Review your recent transactions to see if you’ve missed any qualifying deductions, and consider setting up a dedicated business account if you haven't already. Taking these actions now will make your upcoming tax filing significantly faster and more accurate.
