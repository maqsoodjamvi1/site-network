When I first started collecting crystals, I bought a gorgeous, deep-blue "lapis lazuli" sphere from a flea market table for fifteen dollars. I was thrilled. It wasn't until I dropped it on my hardwood floor a week later—revealing a stark white, chalky plastic core underneath—that I realized I had been scammed. Synthetic, dyed, and completely fake crystals flood the market, and if you are buying stones for their physical beauty or their energetic properties, getting tricked is a frustrating and expensive mistake. 

Over the years, I learned how to separate the natural minerals from the painted glass and molded resin. You do not need a degree in geology or an expensive lab kit to check your stones. You just need to know what physical tests to run right at your kitchen table before you make a purchase.

## 1. The Temperature Test

One of the easiest ways to spot a fake stone is to simply hold it in your hand. Real minerals are thermal conductors, meaning they draw heat away from your skin. 

When you pick up a genuine quartz, jade, or obsidian, it should feel noticeably cold to the touch. It takes a few minutes of holding it tightly in your palm for it to warm up to your body temperature. Glass and plastic, on the other hand, are thermal insulators. They warm up almost instantly because they trap your body heat. If you pick up a smooth, polished sphere and it already feels room-temperature or warm when you first touch it, you are likely holding glass, acrylic, or resin.

## 2. The Weight Check

Fake crystals made of plastic or resin are remarkably light. Natural crystal, being dense mineral matter, has a noticeable heft to it. 

If you suspect a piece might be fake, compare it to a similarly sized piece of kitchen ice, or just use your internal sense of scale. A genuine chunk of pyrite (fool's gold) or hematite will feel surprisingly heavy for its size—almost like a piece of lead. If a large, metallic-looking cluster feels as light as a hollow plastic toy, put it down. Trust your physical instincts; real stone feels solid, dense, and grounded.

## 3. The Close Inspection for Air Bubbles

Grab a magnifying glass or use the macro camera setting on your smartphone to zoom in on the surface and interior of the crystal. This is the ultimate test for glass imitations sold as quartz, citrine, or obsidian.

Natural minerals grow slowly under immense pressure, leaving behind intricate growth lines, internal fractures (sometimes called "veils"), and tiny trapped particles of other minerals. Man-made glass cannot grow this way. Instead, glass-based fakes almost always trap microscopic air bubbles during the melting process. If you look closely inside the stone and see tiny, perfectly round spheres of air trapped beneath the surface, you are definitely looking at colored glass, not a natural gemstone.

## 4. The Scratch Resistance Test

Crystals have varying degrees of hardness, which scientists measure using the Mohs scale. Quartz, for instance, sits at a 7 on the scale, meaning it is harder than common steel and window glass. Plastic is very soft, usually sitting around a 2 or 3.

*   Try to gently scratch the bottom of the crystal with the tip of a steel sewing needle or a pair of metal scissors. (Always do this on a hidden spot, like the flat base of a tower or the bottom of a cluster). 
*   If the metal leaves a powdery grey streak that wipes right off, your crystal is hard. 
*   If the metal actually carves a permanent groove or gouge into the stone, you are dealing with a soft plastic or resin replica. 

Keep in mind that some real stones—like calcite, selenite, and fluorite—are naturally quite soft and *can* be scratched by metal, so use this test primarily for stones marketed as quartz variants.

## 5. The Black Light and Water Clues

There are a few quick specialty checks you can run if you want to be extra thorough. First, watch out for unnatural colors. If a bright neon-blue or emerald-green geode is being sold as a natural wonder, be skeptical. Many agates and quartzes are dyed. You can sometimes spot dye pooling in the natural cracks and crevices of the stone like spilled ink.

You can also use a cheap ultraviolet (UV) blacklight in a dark room. Many natural minerals—such as certain types of calcite, hyalite opal, and ruby—will fluoresce brilliantly under UV light, glowing neon orange, pink, or green. Fakes made of plain glass or plastic generally remain dull and unresponsive to the blacklight. 

Building a collection you trust takes a little bit of practice and skepticism, but running these simple physical checks will save you from wasting money on mass-produced imitations. As you start evaluating your own collection or preparing for your next trip to the local mineral shop, take your time, ask questions, and don't be afraid to hold the stone up to the light before you buy.
