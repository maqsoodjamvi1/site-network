When my oldest son started high school, he lived for his travel soccer team. Practices were Tuesday and Thursday nights, games were all weekend, and he spent his free time watching highlight reels on YouTube. By October of his sophomore year, he suddenly stopped going. He didn’t just quit the team; he stopped watching the clips, stopped texting his teammates, and started spending his evenings staring at his ceiling.

As a parent, your first instinct is to panic. Is this a mental health emergency, or is he just burned out from an overloaded schedule?

Distinguishing between burnout and clinical depression is tricky because they share a primary symptom: a loss of interest in things that used to bring joy. However, the root causes and the paths to recovery are entirely different.

## The "Refillable" Nature of Burnout
Burnout is almost always tied to a specific area of life—school, sports, or a high-pressure hobby. It happens when the demand placed on a teenager exceeds their current capacity to cope. 

Think of burnout like a battery that is drained. If your teen is experiencing burnout, they usually still have the capacity to enjoy things outside of the specific stressor. For example, my son was exhausted by the intensity of soccer, but he still found energy to help me in the garage or grab dinner with a friend. 

When a teen is burned out, they aren't necessarily losing interest in their *life*; they are losing interest in *the specific activity that is draining them*. If you see your teen disengage from their AP classes or their competitive sports team, but they still engage in lower-stakes hobbies—like playing video games with a buddy or drawing in their room—it is likely a sign that they need a reprieve from the pressure, not a clinical intervention.

## The "Pervasiveness" of Clinical Depression
Depression, unlike burnout, is rarely localized. It is a pervasive, heavy fog. If your teen is struggling with clinical depression, the loss of interest—clinically known as anhedonia—is usually total. 

It isn’t just that they don’t want to go to soccer practice; it’s that they don’t want to do *anything*. They stop texting friends they genuinely like. They lose interest in their favorite snacks. Even activities that require zero effort, like watching a movie they’ve seen a dozen times, lose their appeal. 

If you notice a sudden, flat affect across every aspect of their life, that is a red flag. Burnout feels like "I’m too tired to do this," whereas depression feels like "Nothing matters, and I don’t care if I do this or not."

## A Practical Diagnostic Checklist
If you are currently watching your teen pull away, use these three questions to help narrow down what you are looking at:

*   **Is the withdrawal localized?** Ask yourself: Are they still showing engagement in low-pressure settings? If they skip a school event but still eagerly meet a friend for a walk or a smoothie, the issue is likely a specific stressor.
*   **How long has the "flatness" lasted?** Burnout usually happens after a period of intense activity (like finals week or the end of a sports season) and tends to stabilize once the pressure is removed. Depression, however, lingers even when the schedule is wide open.
*   **How do they react to rest?** If you give your teen a "do-nothing" weekend, does that help them recharge? A burned-out teen will show signs of life after a few days of zero responsibilities. A depressed teen often finds that having no responsibilities makes them feel worse, as it provides more time for their internal narrative to spiral.

## What You Can Do This Week
If you suspect burnout, don't rush to a diagnosis. Instead, try a "forced reset." Give them permission to drop one non-essential activity for two weeks. See if their spark returns when the pressure is off. If it does, you have your answer: they are overextended.

If you suspect depression, the approach must change. You aren't looking for a reset; you are looking for support. Don't frame the conversation around their behavior (e.g., "Why are you quitting soccer?"). Frame it around their internal experience (e.g., "I’ve noticed you haven't been hanging out with your friends lately, and you seem like you’re carrying a heavy weight. Tell me what that feels like for you.").

When you start these conversations, your goal is to be a witness, not a problem-solver. If they describe a sense of emptiness that persists regardless of how much sleep or downtime they get, it is time to consult a pediatrician or a mental health professional for an objective assessment. 

Distinguishing between these two states can feel overwhelming, but you know your child better than anyone else. Trust your gut if the change in their demeanor feels "off" or deeper than just being tired. 

Once you have determined whether you are dealing with a temporary case of being overstretched or a deeper emotional struggle, the next steps involve deciding on the right level of professional support or lifestyle adjustment to get your teen back to feeling like themselves.
