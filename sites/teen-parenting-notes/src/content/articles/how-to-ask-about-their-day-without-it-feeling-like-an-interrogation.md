"How was school?" 

If you have a school-aged child, you know the sound of that question hitting the floor. Nine times out of ten, you get a grunt, a shrug, or a flat "fine." It’s not that your kid is being difficult; it’s that the question feels like a performance review. They’ve spent six or seven hours being evaluated, corrected, and managed. When they walk through the door, the last thing they want is a follow-up interrogation.

I spent years frustrated by these dead-end conversations until I realized I was approaching them like a journalist looking for a scoop, rather than a parent just trying to connect. Here is how I shifted my approach to get actual answers—without the attitude.

## Stop asking for a summary
When you ask, "How was your day?" you are asking your child to summarize their entire existence for the last eight hours. That is a massive cognitive load for a kid, especially if they are already mentally exhausted from the school day. 

Instead of a summary, ask for a "data point." Think of it as a low-stakes scavenger hunt. You aren't asking for the narrative arc of their day; you are asking for one specific piece of information.

*   "What was the weirdest thing you saw in the hallway today?"
*   "Did anyone say something that actually made you laugh?"
*   "What was the one thing you were most glad to be finished with?"
*   "Who did you sit with at lunch, and what were they complaining about?"

These questions work because they are specific. They don't require the child to rank their day as "good" or "bad." They just require a tiny observation.

## Use the "Parallel Play" tactic
Often, the pressure of a face-to-face conversation is what kills the dialogue. If you are standing in the kitchen, staring at your child while they are trying to unpack their bag or get a snack, it feels like an interview.

I have found that the best conversations happen when we aren't looking directly at each other. This is the "car talk" phenomenon. When you are driving and your eyes are on the road, your child feels safer opening up because the interaction is less intense. 

You can replicate this at home by doing a chore together. Fold laundry, wipe down the dinner table, or assemble a simple snack. If you are both focused on a task, the conversation can flow incidentally. If the conversation hits a lull, you don't have to scramble to fill the silence; you just keep folding the laundry. That lack of pressure often encourages kids to pick up the thread again once they’ve gathered their thoughts.

## Offer your own "bad" data first
Kids often clamor for privacy because they feel like they are the only ones being put under the microscope. If you want them to share, you have to model the kind of sharing you are looking for.

Don't give them a highlight reel of your day, though. If you tell them, "I had a great day, I got so much work done," you’ve effectively closed the door on them. Instead, share a small, relatable failure or a minor annoyance.

"I had a meeting today where I totally misunderstood the instructions, and I felt like a total goofball. Did anyone have a 'goofball' moment at school today?"

By exposing your own vulnerability, you signal that this conversation isn't a report card—it's a place where we talk about the messy, human parts of the day.

## The "One-to-Ten" scale trick
Sometimes, your child genuinely doesn't have the words to describe how they are feeling. They might be overwhelmed, overstimulated, or just "done." 

When they seem closed off, skip the open-ended questions entirely. Use a scale. 

"On a scale of one to ten, how much was today a 'brain-fry' day?"

If they say "a nine," you know you shouldn't press for details. You know they need space, a snack, and silence. If they say "a four," you can follow up with, "Okay, a four—not too bad, but not a snooze fest. What was the most annoying part of it?" 

This gives them a structured way to communicate their energy level without having to craft a complex sentence.

## Knowing when to retreat
The most important part of "not interrogating" is knowing when to stop. If you have tried a specific question, tried a distraction, and offered a vulnerable story of your own, and you are still getting one-word answers, leave it alone. 

Some days are just hard. Sometimes a child just needs to decompress in their room with a book or a screen. If you push, you’ll be labeled as the person who always nags, and they will start avoiding you at the door. If you respect their need for quiet, they are significantly more likely to come find you at 8:00 PM when their guard is down and they are finally ready to talk.

It isn't about getting the report; it's about keeping the connection open. If you can move from being an interviewer to being a teammate who is just checking in, you’ll find that the stories start coming out on their own, often at the times you least expect.

As you look to refine how you connect with your child during these daily transitions, consider how your own current rhythms might be helping or hindering those open lines of communication. Establishing a few simple, non-negotiable rituals can help take the friction out of the end-of-day routine.
