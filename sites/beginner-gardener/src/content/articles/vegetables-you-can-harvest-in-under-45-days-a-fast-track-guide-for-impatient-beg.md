If you are the type of gardener who wants to see results before your motivation wanes, you are in the right place. Most seed packets promise a harvest in 60 to 90 days, which feels like an eternity when you are staring at a patch of bare dirt in May. 

The secret to a fast harvest is choosing crops that are biologically programmed to mature quickly, rather than trying to force slow-growing plants like peppers or tomatoes to speed up. By focusing on leafy greens and specific root vegetables, you can go from seed to salad bowl in less than seven weeks.

## Radishes: The 25-Day Speedsters
Radishes are the undisputed kings of the "instant gratification" garden. If you want to see a sprout within three days of planting, buy a packet of 'Cherry Belle' or 'French Breakfast' radish seeds. 

Here is the exact method I use: clear a small patch of soil, rake it until it is fine and crumbly, and poke holes about half an inch deep. Drop one seed every two inches, cover them lightly, and water them gently with a misting nozzle so you don’t wash the seeds away. 

The trick here is thinning. If you crowd them, they will grow beautiful leaves but tiny, woody roots. Once the seedlings have their first set of "true leaves" (the ones that look like mature radish leaves), pull out every other plant. You can actually eat those tiny thinned sprouts—they taste like spicy microgreens. If you water them every single day, you will be pulling crisp, red radishes out of the ground in exactly 25 to 30 days.

## Baby Leaf Lettuce and Arugula
If you buy "head lettuce" seeds, you are signing up for a 60-day commitment. Instead, look for seed packets labeled "baby leaf" or "cut-and-come-again." 

I don't bother planting these in neat rows anymore. I prepare a 1x2 foot container or a small raised bed section, sprinkle the seeds thick across the surface like I’m seasoning a steak, and lightly dust them with a quarter-inch of potting mix. 

By day 21, you will have a miniature forest of greens. You don't have to wait for the whole plant to mature. Take a pair of kitchen scissors and snip the outer leaves about an inch above the soil line. If you leave the central crown intact, the plant will keep growing, and you can harvest from the same patch two or three times before the summer heat makes them turn bitter.

## Green Onions (Scallions)
Most people think onions are a long-season crop that takes all summer. That is true for large bulb onions, but green onions are a different story. If you start from seed, they can take a while, but there is a "cheat code" for the impatient gardener: buy a bunch of organic green onions from the grocery store.

Take the white ends (the root side), trim the green tops for your dinner, and plant the white stubs directly into the soil. Leave about an inch of the white part exposed above the dirt. Within two weeks, you will have brand-new green stalks shooting up. You have essentially skipped the germination phase entirely. This is the fastest way to get fresh onions in the kitchen, and they will continue to provide clippings for months if you keep the soil moist.

## Spinach for the Fast Track
Spinach is a cool-weather crop. If you live in a region that gets hot quickly, you only have a small window to get these in the ground. I prefer the 'Bloomsdale' variety because it is hardy and grows fast.

Like lettuce, you can harvest spinach as "baby spinach" in about 30 to 35 days. The key is consistent moisture. If the soil dries out, spinach will "bolt," which means it will immediately send up a flower stalk and turn the leaves bitter and tough. If you are a forgetful waterer, use a layer of straw mulch around the base of the plants to lock the moisture in.

## A Quick Reference for Your Garden Journal
To keep your momentum going, keep this list of reliable performers handy when you hit the nursery or shop online:

*   **Radishes:** 25-30 days (Harvest when the shoulder of the root pokes through the soil).
*   **Arugula:** 30 days (Harvest before the leaves get too large; they get spicier as they age).
*   **Baby Leaf Lettuce:** 21-35 days (Snip, don't pull).
*   **Turnips (for greens):** 30 days (Harvest the tops long before the root reaches full size).
*   **Green Onions:** 14-21 days (Using the store-bought root method).

## Managing Your Expectations
The biggest mistake beginners make is planting everything on the same day. If you plant 50 radish seeds at once, you will have 50 radishes ready on the exact same Tuesday. You won't be able to eat them all, and they will eventually turn pithy and woody in the ground.

Instead, practice "succession planting." Plant a small pinch of seeds every ten days. This ensures that you have a steady supply of fresh vegetables coming in, rather than a single, overwhelming harvest that you can’t keep up with.

Once you have your first harvest in the basket, you will have a better feel for your soil and how much water your specific patch needs. Use the success of these fast-growing crops as your foundation for trying slightly longer-term vegetables later in the season.
