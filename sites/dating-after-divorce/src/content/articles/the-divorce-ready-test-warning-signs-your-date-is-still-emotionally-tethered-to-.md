When you start dating someone new, the last thing you want to do is play detective. You want to focus on chemistry, shared hobbies, and whether they make you laugh. But if you’ve noticed little red flags—like your date mentioning their ex during a romantic dinner or keeping a box of photos on their nightstand—you might be worried that they aren't fully available.

Emotional tethering isn't always about someone still being in love with their ex. Often, it’s about unfinished business, unresolved anger, or an inability to close the door on the past. If you’re getting the sense that you’re dating someone who is "divorce-ready" (meaning they are technically single but emotionally married to their previous dynamic), look for these concrete behaviors.

## The Comparison Trap in Daily Life
The biggest indicator that your date is still tethered is when their ex is the "gold standard" for everything, whether for good or bad. 

If they constantly bring up how their ex liked their coffee, how their ex handled household chores, or how their ex reacted to stressful situations, they are still using that person as a baseline. It doesn’t matter if they are speaking fondly or venomously. If the ex is still the main character in the stories they tell about their present life, you are essentially the guest star in someone else’s drama.

**Watch for these specific verbal cues:**
*   **The "We" slip:** They accidentally refer to themselves and their ex as "we" when talking about future plans or past routines.
*   **The unsolicited defense:** If you make a neutral comment about a movie or a restaurant, and they immediately jump to defend or attack their ex’s taste, it shows that the ex is still the filter through which they see the world.
*   **The "fix-it" project:** They talk about their ex’s current problems as if they are still responsible for solving them. If your date is still acting as a consultant for their ex’s career, finances, or family issues, their energy is being drained by a relationship that should have been decommissioned.

## Logistics as a Smoke Screen
Sometimes, people stay tethered under the guise of "logistics." They might claim they have to stay in touch because of shared property, lingering legal paperwork, or mutual friend groups. While some of these things are unavoidable after a breakup or divorce, the way they handle these logistics tells the real story.

Ask yourself: Do they handle these issues with clinical, business-like detachment, or is there a frantic, emotional charge behind every interaction? 

If they are texting their ex at 10:00 PM to discuss a minor utility bill or checking their ex’s social media daily to "see how they’re doing with the breakup," that is an emotional tether. A person who is actually moved on treats these administrative tasks like a dental appointment—necessary, boring, and something they want to get over with as quickly as possible. If they are making it a hobby, they are holding on.

## The "Secret Keeper" Syndrome
Pay attention to what they hide from you. If your date is deleting texts before you sit down for dinner, or if they act visibly jumpy when their phone pings, they are managing their relationship with their ex in a way that excludes you.

Transparency is the antidote to the "divorce-ready" problem. If you ask a simple, calm question like, "Are you still in regular touch with your ex?" and they react with defensiveness, gaslighting, or extreme secrecy, take that as a data point. They aren't ready to invite you into their full life because their full life is still being shared with someone else.

## Testing the Waters
If you aren't sure where the line is, try a "reality check" conversation. Don't frame it as an accusation. Instead, talk about your own boundaries regarding exes. Say something like, "I’ve realized I’m most comfortable dating people who have clear, low-contact boundaries with their former partners so we can focus on building something new. Where do you stand on that?"

Observe their reaction:
*   **The healthy partner:** Will validate your boundary and explain how they’ve created distance to prioritize their own growth.
*   **The tethered partner:** Will often call you "jealous," "insecure," or "controlling." They will focus on why their situation is "different" or "complicated."

Complexity is a choice, not a circumstance. While divorce and breakups are difficult, keeping a healthy distance is a skill that available adults prioritize. If they aren't willing to protect the space between you and their past, they aren't offering you a clean slate—they’re offering you a seat at an already crowded table.

Evaluating these signs early on can save you months of emotional labor. You deserve a partner who is fully present and excited to build a future with you, rather than one who is busy looking over their shoulder at the ghost of a relationship that should have stayed in the past. 

As you consider whether this person is truly available for the commitment you’re looking for, think about the energy you want to invest in this budding connection. Deciding how to handle these observations is the next step in protecting your own peace.
