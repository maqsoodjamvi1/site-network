When you return to the dating scene after a long marriage, the temptation to use your profile as a confession booth is high. You might feel like you need to explain the "why" behind your current status to justify your presence on an app. You might think that by mentioning your divorce, you are filtering out people who aren't looking for something serious or scaring away those who might be intimidated by your past.

In reality, your profile is not a legal deposition. It is a billboard. If you spend your limited character count explaining the end of your marriage, you aren’t telling potential dates who you are today; you are telling them who you were yesterday. Here is how to navigate the digital dating transition by focusing on the present, not the paperwork.

## Focus on Current Interests, Not Past Roles

When you were married, your identity was often tied to your household, your children, or your role as a partner. Now that you are single, you need to re-establish your individual identity. If your profile reads like a description of a former family unit—or worse, a list of things you are "ready to leave behind"—you will attract people who want to fix you rather than date you.

Instead of writing, "I’m getting back out there after 15 years of marriage and looking to rebuild my life," try focusing on your current hobbies. If you’ve started taking pottery classes, hiking on weekends, or reading sci-fi novels, lead with those. 

**Example:**
*   **Instead of:** "Starting over after a long marriage and looking for someone who understands."
*   **Try:** "Passionate about wood-fired pizza making and finding the best hiking trails in the county. Currently training for a 5K and looking for someone to join me for the finish line celebration."

This shift moves the focus from your status as a "divorced person" to your identity as a "person with interests."

## The "Must-Have" List vs. The "Must-Avoid" List

Many people who have been through a divorce fall into the trap of using their profile to list their grievances. You might see profiles that say, "No liars, no cheaters, and no games." While your boundaries are valid, these statements are a red flag to healthy potential partners. They signal that you are still carrying the emotional baggage of your previous relationship and that you are approaching dating from a place of defense rather than curiosity.

If you have requirements, frame them positively. If you want someone who is honest, describe yourself as someone who values direct communication. If you want someone who is reliable, describe your own love for planning weekend trips or keeping a consistent gym routine. 

By detailing what you bring to the table rather than what you are trying to escape, you change the energy of your profile from "wounded" to "ready."

## Audit Your Photos for Timeline Clues

Your photos are often the first thing people look at, and they are the easiest place to accidentally reveal your past. If you are using photos from five years ago because you haven't taken new ones, you are creating a disconnect. If you are using photos where you’ve cropped someone else out—leaving a mysterious floating arm or a cropped-off shoulder—you are sending a subtle message that you are still editing your history.

Take the time to curate a fresh set of images. You don't need a professional photoshoot, but you do need:
*   **One clear, high-resolution headshot:** No sunglasses, no hats. 
*   **Two action shots:** Showing you doing something you enjoy, like gardening, cooking, or walking your dog.
*   **One full-body shot:** This helps avoid the "catfish" anxiety that people often feel when meeting someone new.

If you don't have these, ask a friend to take them this weekend. A photo of you in a new environment—even if it’s just your favorite local coffee shop—signals that your life is moving forward.

## Use Your "About Me" to Start a Conversation

The best profile is one that gives a stranger an easy way to say "hello." If you mention your divorce, you are inviting people to ask, "So, what happened?" That is a heavy conversation to have before you’ve even ordered a drink. 

Instead, provide "hooks" for a conversation. If you mention that you love traveling to national parks, a match can ask, "Which one is your favorite?" If you mention that you are a fan of 80s rock, they can ask about your favorite concert. 

**Hooks to include:**
*   A recent achievement you’re proud of (e.g., "Just finished learning how to make homemade sourdough").
*   A specific, low-stakes debate (e.g., "I will die on the hill that the original ending of the movie was better").
*   A local recommendation you need (e.g., "Looking for the best hidden gem restaurant in the city").

When you provide these hooks, you move the interaction into the present moment. You allow your match to get to know you as you are right now, rather than as a person defined by your previous chapter.

By removing the references to your past, you aren't hiding your life experience; you are prioritizing your future. As you prepare to hit "publish" on your updated profile, think about the kind of person you want to meet. If you want to meet someone who is excited about their life and looking for a genuine connection, show them that you are exactly that person. Once your profile is polished and reflective of your current self, you’ll be much better positioned to handle the conversations that follow.
