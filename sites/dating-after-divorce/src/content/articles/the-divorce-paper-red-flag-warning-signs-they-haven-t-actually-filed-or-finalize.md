When you start dating someone new, the "divorce status" conversation is one of the most awkward parts of the vetting process. Many people are genuinely separated, living in different homes, and working through the legal slog of ending a marriage. However, there is a specific category of partners who claim they are "divorced" or "finalizing the paperwork" when, in reality, they haven't even filed a petition.

I have seen too many friends get three, six, or twelve months into a relationship only to find out that their partner is still legally married and, more importantly, still emotionally or financially tied to their spouse. Here is how to look past the excuses and verify the reality of their situation.

## Why People Lie About Their Divorce Status
It’s rarely as simple as a malicious plan to trick you. Often, people want to move on, but the legal reality is expensive and exhausting. They might be "separated" in their head, but if they haven't retained a lawyer or filed with the court, they are still married. 

Some people lie because they fear that being "married" will disqualify them from being taken seriously in the dating pool. They tell themselves that the marriage is "basically over," so saying they are single feels like a minor technicality. To you, however, it’s a major risk. If they haven’t filed, they can change their mind, reconcile, or get blindsided by a spouse who decides to contest the divorce and drain their joint bank accounts—which now directly impacts your life.

## The Subtle Warning Signs
If a date tells you they are divorced or "just finalizing," watch for these specific behaviors that suggest the paperwork hasn't even been started:

* **The "Privacy" Shield:** If you ask a follow-up question—like, "When did you file?" or "Who is your attorney?"—and they get defensive or call you "nosy" or "judgmental," take note. A person who is actually in the middle of a legal divorce will usually be happy to vent about the process or answer basic questions because it is a massive, stressful part of their current life.
* **The Financial Wall:** If you are at the stage where you are splitting costs (dinners, travel, living expenses) and they cannot pay because their "money is tied up in the divorce," be careful. This is often a sign of a looming legal battle they haven't disclosed.
* **The "Living Together" Mismatch:** If they claim they are finalizing the divorce but still have keys to the marital home, go there to pick up mail, or maintain a joint bank account for "convenience," they are not separated in any functional sense. 
* **The Lack of Court Documents:** If they claim it’s "in the final stages" but can’t tell you the name of the county or the court where it is being processed, they are likely stalling.

## How to Verify Without Being a Private Investigator
You don’t need to be a stalker to protect yourself. In the United States, divorce records are almost always public information. You can do a "public records search" for the county where they live.

Search the local Clerk of Court website for that county. Most have a "Case Search" or "Court Records" portal. Enter their full legal name. If they are truly in the middle of a divorce, you will see a case number, a filing date, and a docket of recent activity. If you search the county and find absolutely nothing, they likely haven’t filed. 

If they live in a state where records aren't easily searchable online, you can call the Clerk of Court’s office. You don't need a lawyer to do this; you just need their full legal name and date of birth. Ask: "I’m looking to see if there is an active divorce case for [Name] in this jurisdiction." They will usually give you a yes or no answer.

## What to Do When You Find the Truth
If you discover they haven't filed, don't necessarily jump straight to breaking up—unless the lying itself is a dealbreaker. Instead, have a direct conversation. Use a "bridge" approach: "I checked the court records because I felt like you were being vague, and I didn't see a case filed. I value honesty over everything else, so I need to know why you told me you were divorced when the legal process hasn't started."

Listen to how they respond. If they blame their spouse, the legal system, or you for "checking up on them," they aren't ready for a healthy relationship. If they own it, apologize, and explain their fear of being judged, you have a baseline to decide if you want to wait for them to actually start the process.

## Protecting Your Future
Being with someone who isn't legally disentangled from their past creates a "third party" in your relationship. Their legal, financial, and emotional baggage remains attached to their ex-spouse. If they aren't willing to be transparent about the legal state of their marriage, they are likely not ready to be transparent about the emotional state of your relationship.

Once you have gathered the facts, you need to decide if you are comfortable dating someone who is still legally bound to someone else. Before you commit to a deeper level of intimacy or combine your lives in any way, consider what your next move should be regarding the future of your dating life.
