The term "Sunday Scaries" has become a cultural shorthand for the dread that kicks in around 4:00 p.m. on a weekend. But for many, this isn't just a mental state; it is a physiological alarm system. If you find yourself physically rattled before the work week begins, your nervous system is likely trapped in a cycle of chronic "fight or flight."

When we talk about being overworked, we usually point to a heavy inbox or a looming project deadline. However, your body often recognizes the threat of the coming week long before your brain consciously acknowledges the stress. Here are five specific, subtle physical symptoms that your nervous system is signaling it has reached its threshold.

## 1. The Afternoon "Jaw Reset"
Do you catch yourself with your teeth clenched together, or find that you’ve been pressing your tongue against the roof of your mouth for the last twenty minutes? This is a classic sign of sympathetic nervous system activation. 

When your body senses a threat—even if that threat is just a calendar full of meetings—it prepares for impact. It tightens the jaw muscles to protect the airway and prepare for physical defense. If you notice your jaw aching on a Sunday evening, don't just reach for a painkiller. Instead, try the "physiological sigh": take two sharp, consecutive inhales through your nose, followed by a long, slow exhale through your mouth. Do this three times. If your jaw drops open or feels "heavy" afterward, you’ve successfully signaled to your brain that the immediate threat has passed.

## 2. Low-Level Digestive "Humming"
Many people associate stress with acute stomach pain, but the nervous system expresses overwhelm more subtly through what I call digestive humming. This manifests as a faint, persistent feeling of nausea, or a sense that your stomach is "vibrating" or unsettled, even if you haven't eaten anything suspicious. 

This happens because your body is diverting blood flow away from your gut—which is considered "non-essential" during a stress response—and toward your muscles. If you’re feeling this on a Sunday night, stop trying to eat through the sensation. Instead, drink a room-temperature glass of water slowly while standing or sitting upright. The goal is to move out of the "rest and digest" suppression mode by physically grounding your posture.

## 3. The "Eye-Strain" Illusion
Have you ever felt like your vision is slightly blurred, or that you cannot quite focus your eyes on a book or a screen, even though your vision is perfectly fine? This is called "foveal vision narrowing." 

Under stress, your nervous system forces you to focus on a narrow point ahead of you, as if you are tracking a predator. This constant focus creates a sensation of eye strain that feels physical, even if you haven't been looking at a screen all day. To counteract this, practice "panoramic vision." While sitting in your living room, keep your head still and try to notice the walls to your left and right simultaneously without moving your eyes. By forcing your gaze to expand, you physically override the nervous system’s narrow-focus emergency setting.

## 4. Unexplained Temperature Fluctuations
Does your skin suddenly feel "prickly," or do you find yourself cycling between feeling freezing cold and then suddenly flushed, even while sitting in a room with a stable temperature? This is your autonomic nervous system struggling to regulate your baseline.

When the nervous system is overworked, it loses its ability to manage small physiological tasks like thermoregulation efficiently. If you feel a sudden wave of heat or a chill that doesn't match the environment, take off one layer of clothing or put on a sweater. Changing your physical environment by just a few degrees can help "reset" the sensory feedback loop your brain is currently stuck in.

## 5. The "Hanging" Breath
We often think we are breathing normally, but check in with your ribcage right now. Are you taking short, shallow breaths into your upper chest? Many people hold their breath for several seconds at a time—a phenomenon called "email apnea"—without realizing it. 

If you notice your breath feels "stuck" or shallow on a Sunday evening, you are likely in a state of hyper-arousal. The fix here isn't deep breathing, which can actually feel like another chore to complete. Instead, focus entirely on the exhale. Let your breath out until your lungs are completely empty, then let the inhale happen naturally without forcing it. Doing this for sixty seconds helps reset your carbon dioxide tolerance and quiets the nervous system’s need to stay on high alert.

***

Recognizing these symptoms is the first step toward reclaiming your Sunday evenings. You cannot "think" your way out of a nervous system that is already in survival mode; you have to use physical cues to prove to your body that you are currently safe. 

As you start to notice these specific signals, the next step is identifying which ones are your "early warnings." We will look at how to build a short, five-minute reset routine that targets these specific physical triggers before they escalate into full-blown anxiety.
