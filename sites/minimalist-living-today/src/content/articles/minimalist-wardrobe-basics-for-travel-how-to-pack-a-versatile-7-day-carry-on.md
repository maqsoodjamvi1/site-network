I spent the first decade of my adult life traveling with a massive, hard-shell suitcase that I checked for every flight. I dragged it over cobblestones in Europe, shoved it into cramped rental car trunks, and spent half of every vacation worrying if the airline had lost it. Three years ago, I switched to a strict one-bag minimalist approach. I haven’t checked a bag since.

If you are intimidated by the idea of fitting a week’s worth of clothing into a carry-on, the secret isn't folding techniques—it’s the math of your wardrobe. Here is how I build a 7-day travel kit that handles everything from a business dinner to a rainy afternoon hike.

## The Rule of Three for Footwear

Shoes are the biggest space-wasters in a suitcase. For a seven-day trip, I never pack more than two pairs, and I wear the bulkiest pair on the plane.

If I’m traveling for a mix of city walking and a nice dinner, I wear a pair of clean, white leather sneakers (like a minimalist tennis shoe) on the plane. These look sharp enough for a restaurant but are comfortable for ten miles of walking. In my bag, I pack a pair of lightweight, packable loafers or a slim Chelsea boot. 

If your trip requires a workout, replace the secondary shoe with a pair of minimal running shoes. Avoid "do-it-all" shoes that are ugly; you’ll end up feeling self-conscious in them. Instead, choose two distinct pairs that each serve a specific function.

## Selecting Your "Core" Palette

The biggest mistake people make is packing "outfits" instead of "components." If you pack a specific shirt that only matches one pair of pants, you have wasted precious space.

I stick to a strict color palette: navy, grey, and white. Every item I pack must match every other item. When you stick to a neutral palette, you can create a new look every day just by swapping a layer. 

For a 7-day trip, my packing list looks like this:
*   **Bottoms:** Two pairs of chinos or high-quality technical trousers (wear one, pack one).
*   **Tops:** Four t-shirts (mix of grey and white), one long-sleeve base layer, and one button-down shirt.
*   **Layers:** One mid-weight sweater or a zip-up fleece.
*   **Outerwear:** One lightweight, water-resistant jacket (wear this on the plane to save space).
*   **Undergarments:** Five pairs of merino wool underwear and five pairs of thin, merino wool socks.

I don’t pack seven shirts for seven days. Merino wool is the secret weapon of the minimalist traveler. It is naturally odor-resistant and moisture-wicking. You can wear a merino shirt two or three times before it needs a wash, and it dries overnight in a hotel sink. 

## The Art of the "Sink Wash"

Minimalism only works if you are willing to maintain your wardrobe while on the road. I carry a small, two-ounce bottle of travel-sized detergent. 

Every other night, I wash my socks and underwear in the hotel sink. I roll them tightly in a microfiber towel to wring out the moisture, then hang them over the shower rod. By the time I wake up, they are dry. This habit allows me to carry a fraction of the clothing that a typical traveler brings, leaving me with a bag that weighs less than 15 pounds.

## Utilizing Compression Cubes

I used to think packing cubes were just for organized people, but they are actually about volume reduction. I use two specific compression cubes. 

In the first cube, I put my shirts and the button-down. In the second, I put my socks and underwear. The compression zipper squeezes the air out of the clothing, turning a bulky stack of cotton into a dense, flat brick. 

When I arrive at my destination, I don’t unpack the whole bag. I leave the cubes in the suitcase and pull out exactly what I need for the day. This keeps my hotel room from becoming a disaster zone of loose clothes and helps me repack in under three minutes when it’s time to head to the airport.

## Why This Changes Your Trip

The goal of this method isn't to live with less for the sake of suffering; it’s to eliminate the friction of travel. When your bag weighs 12 pounds, you don't worry about airline fees. You don't have to wait at the baggage claim carousel. You can hop on a subway or walk to your hotel without needing an expensive taxi. 

You will find that having fewer choices actually makes your mornings easier. When every shirt you own on the trip works with every pair of pants, you stop staring at your suitcase and start heading out the door to see the city.

The next step is to pull out the clothes you think you need for your upcoming trip and lay them on your bed. Take away the "just in case" items—the extra jacket you think you might need for a surprise blizzard or the third pair of shoes. Once you have your core components narrowed down, you are ready to start packing for your first truly minimalist adventure.
