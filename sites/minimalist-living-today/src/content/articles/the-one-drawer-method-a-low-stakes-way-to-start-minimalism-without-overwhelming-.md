When I first decided to try minimalism, I made the mistake of dumping my entire bedroom closet onto the floor. I spent six hours staring at a pile of shirts I hadn’t worn since 2016, felt guilty about the money I’d wasted, and ended up shoving everything back into the closet out of pure exhaustion. 

The next day, I was exactly where I started, but with a lot more dust bunnies.

If you are intimidated by the idea of decluttering your whole house, you aren't alone. Most of the "minimalist" advice online suggests extreme overhauls that require weeks of free time and a high tolerance for mess. But if you have a job, a family, or just a life that doesn't revolve around organizing, that approach is a recipe for failure.

Instead, I suggest the "One-Drawer" method. It is the lowest-stakes way to test the minimalist philosophy without committing to a full lifestyle change.

## Choose Your Single Square Foot

The core of this method is simple: Pick one drawer in your home. Not a closet, not a room, and definitely not the garage. Just one drawer.

It needs to be a drawer you interact with every single day—the "junk" drawer in the kitchen, your bedside table, or the drawer where you keep your pens and chargers. The goal isn't to fix your entire storage system; it’s to prove to yourself that you can get rid of things you don't need without your life falling apart.

Pick that drawer right now. Don't overthink it. Once you have it, clear your schedule for exactly 15 minutes. That is all the time you need.

## The Three-Pile Rule

Empty the entire contents of that drawer onto a table or a clean section of the floor. Do not leave anything inside the drawer. Once it’s empty, wipe it down. There is a psychological benefit to starting with a clean, empty slate.

Now, pick up every single item. You are going to sort these items into three piles. Be ruthless, but be logical:

*   **The Keepers:** These are items you have used in the last 30 days. If you haven't touched it in a month, it is not a keeper. 
*   **The Relocators:** These are things that shouldn't be in this drawer at all. Maybe you found a spare battery that belongs in the utility closet, or a set of keys that belongs in the hallway bowl. Don't try to organize your whole house; just put these in a box to be moved later.
*   **The Go-Aways:** This is where the magic happens. These are the dried-up pens, the menus for restaurants that closed three years ago, the tangled cords for electronics you no longer own, and the instruction manuals for appliances you threw away a decade ago. 

## The "Trash vs. Donate" Filter

Before you put your "Keepers" back, look at your "Go-Aways" pile. If it is broken, dried out, or technically trash, throw it in the bin immediately. Don't worry about being "wasteful." If an item is useless, it is already waste; keeping it in your drawer is just storing trash.

If you have items that are functional but you just don't use—like a kitchen gadget you bought on a whim—put those in a box labeled "Donate." Do not let these items linger on your counter or in your hallway. If you don't take them to a donation center within 48 hours, they will eventually migrate back into your drawers.

## Why This Works Long-Term

The reason the One-Drawer method succeeds where "whole-house decluttering" fails is the feedback loop. When you open that drawer tomorrow morning to grab a pen or a set of keys, you will feel a tiny jolt of satisfaction. Because there is less clutter, you can find exactly what you need in under two seconds. 

You aren't just cleaning a drawer; you are training your brain to prefer a state of "less."

Once you experience that ease, you will start looking at the drawer next to it with a different perspective. You’ll realize that the extra space is more valuable than the random clutter you were hoarding. You don't need a massive plan or a professional organizer to become a minimalist. You just need to reclaim one small, manageable space at a time. 

If you successfully maintain your one drawer for a week, you have officially begun the process of auditing your environment. The next logical step is to address the surfaces in your home—the tabletops and counters—where the overflow from your drawers usually ends up. Focus on keeping your one drawer empty of "junk" for another week, and you’ll find that the desire to clear the rest of your space will start to happen naturally, rather than feeling like a chore you have to force yourself to do.
