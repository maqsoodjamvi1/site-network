When I first started training my rescue dog, I made the mistake of teaching "stay" before anything else. I thought it was the ultimate test of obedience. What I learned the hard way was that "stay" is a heavy-duty, static command that is often too formal for daily life. 

Most people don't need their dog to sit in a statue-like pose for five minutes while they go to the grocery store. What they actually need is for their dog to pause for three seconds so they can open a door without getting knocked over. That is where "wait" comes in. 

If you are just starting out with a puppy or a new rescue, I recommend teaching "wait" before you ever touch "stay." Here is why it makes a difference and how you can do it today.

## The Functional Difference
The biggest issue with "stay" is that it usually implies a specific position—your dog must remain in a sit or a down until you return and release them. It’s a rigid instruction. 

"Wait," on the other hand, is flexible. It simply means "don't move forward right now." When I tell my dog to "wait" at the top of the stairs, I don’t care if he’s standing, sitting, or lying down. I just care that he doesn't descend until I give the go-ahead. It’s a casual, "pause" button for your dog’s movement. 

Because "wait" is less demanding, dogs tend to learn it faster and with less frustration. It builds impulse control without the pressure of forcing them into a static posture, which is helpful if your dog is high-energy or easily bored.

## Why "Wait" is the Better Doorway Tool
The most common scenario where people struggle with their dogs is the front door. We’ve all seen the dog that bolts the second the door cracks open. 

If you try to use "stay" at the door, you have to command a sit, maintain eye contact, reach for the handle, open it, and keep the dog pinned in place. That is a lot of moving parts for a dog to manage. 

With "wait," you aren't asking for a sit. You are simply asking for a momentary pause. 
1. Ask for a "wait" while you are two feet away from the door.
2. Slowly reach for the handle. If your dog steps forward, gently close the door (just an inch) to reset. 
3. Open the door again. If they hold their position, praise them.
4. Give a release word like "free" or "go" to signal that they can now cross the threshold.

By practicing this, you are teaching them that the door opening is not an invitation to bolt; it is a cue to look to you for permission.

## Step-by-Step: The "Wait" Training Plan
You don't need a formal training session to teach this. You can do it during your regular routine. Here is a simple progression I use:

* **The Mealtime Foundation:** Dogs are most motivated by food. When you go to put their bowl down, ask them to "wait." If they move toward the bowl, lift it back up. Don't say anything; just lift the bowl. When they sit or stand still for even a second, place the bowl down. Use a release word like "okay" to let them eat.
* **The Threshold Practice:** Use the door method mentioned above. Start with the door only cracked open. As your dog gets better at waiting, open the door wider. 
* **The Car Exit:** This is a safety essential. Never let your dog jump out of the car the moment you open the trunk or door. Make them "wait" until you have the leash attached and you are ready for them to move. This prevents them from darting into a busy parking lot.
* **The Toy Toss:** If you play fetch, keep the ball in your hand. Ask for a "wait." Toss the ball, but don't let them go immediately. Keep them there for three seconds, then release them. This teaches them that the reward comes after the pause.

## Keep It Simple and Consistent
The biggest trap people fall into is repeating the word. If you say, "Wait, wait, wait, wait," your dog stops hearing it. They just hear white noise. Say the word once, clearly, and then use your body language to back it up. If they break the wait, quietly guide them back to their starting point and try again. 

Consistency is the secret sauce. If you allow them to push past you at the door on Tuesday because you're in a hurry, they will ignore the command on Wednesday. If you’re in a rush, put them in a crate or behind a baby gate rather than testing a command they haven't mastered yet.

Once your dog understands that "wait" consistently leads to a reward—like getting their food, going outside, or getting the ball—they will start offering the behavior automatically. They will start pausing at doorways or before their meals, checking in with you to see if they have the green light. 

Building this foundation of impulse control makes the transition to more formal commands like "stay" significantly easier later on, because your dog already understands the concept of holding their position on your terms.

Now that you have a clear understanding of why "wait" is the most practical starting point for your dog's training, you can begin applying these methods to your daily routines immediately. Focus on being patient during these first few sessions, as consistent small wins will build a much stronger bond than high-pressure drills.
