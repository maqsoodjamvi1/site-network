I used to spend my Sunday nights feeling a low-level hum of anxiety. I’d be sitting on the couch, staring at the television, but my brain was already doing inventory on the Monday morning meeting I wasn't prepared for or the fact that my kitchen was a disaster zone. I realized that my weekends were essentially evaporating because I was using them to recover from chaos rather than actually resting.

Over the last few years, I’ve shifted my approach. I don't believe in "productivity hacks" that turn your day off into a chore list. Instead, I use a Sunday Reset. This isn’t a deep clean of your home; it’s a series of five micro-habits designed to offload the mental clutter so that Monday morning feels like a transition rather than a collision.

## 1. The "Inbox Zero" Sweep of Your Physical Space
We often focus on our email inboxes, but our physical surfaces are usually the source of Monday morning friction. I spend exactly 15 minutes doing a "perimeter sweep" of my main living area. 

I’m not talking about scrubbing floors. I’m talking about removing the things that don’t belong in the room. If there’s a stack of mail on the kitchen island, a stray pair of shoes in the hallway, or a pile of clothes on the "chair" (we all have that one chair), I put them back in their home. The goal is to walk into your kitchen on Monday morning and find a clear counter. When your environment is visually calm, your brain has one less thing to process before you’ve even had your coffee.

## 2. The "Outfit Pre-Flight"
Decision fatigue is real, and it hits hardest at 7:00 AM on a Monday. I have a rule: if I’m going to leave the house, the clothes have to be chosen, steamed (if necessary), and laid out on Sunday night. 

This includes everything. If I’m wearing socks, they are placed on top of the shoes. If I’m wearing a necklace, it’s hanging off the hanger. When I wake up, I don’t have to think about whether these pants match this shirt or if the item is wrinkled. I just put on the clothes and move to the kitchen. It saves me about ten minutes of aimless staring at my closet, which is ten minutes I can spend actually waking up.

## 3. The Sunday Night Brain Dump
I keep a small physical notebook by my bed, but you can use a notes app on your phone. Before I get into bed, I write down the three most important things I need to do on Monday. 

Crucially, these aren't "catch-up" tasks. They are concrete, actionable items. Instead of writing "Work on project," I write "Finish the first two slides for the project presentation." By externalizing these tasks onto paper, I’m giving my brain permission to stop looping on them. I’ve found that when I write down my "top three" for the next day, I sleep significantly better because I’m not spending the night wondering if I’m forgetting something important.

## 4. The "Tech Sunset" at 8:00 PM
This is the hardest habit to stick to, but it has the highest ROI. At 8:00 PM, I put my phone in a dedicated drawer in the kitchen. It stays there until morning. 

If I have my phone in bed, I inevitably end up on social media or checking work emails, which keeps my brain in "problem-solving mode" long after it should be resting. When the phone is in the kitchen, I spend the last hour of my night reading a physical book, stretching, or just listening to a podcast. Removing the blue light and the constant stream of notifications helps my nervous system move into a restorative state, making it much easier to fall asleep.

## 5. The "Monday Morning Ritual" Setup
I want my first 30 minutes of Monday to be as pleasant as possible. I prepare the environment to serve me. I fill the water tank of the coffee maker and put the grounds in the filter. I set my favorite mug next to the machine. I make sure my water bottle is filled and sitting by the front door. 

These are tiny actions that take less than two minutes. However, when I stumble into the kitchen in the dark, the fact that I don’t have to hunt for a clean mug or wait for the machine to be set up makes me feel like I’m taking care of myself. It’s a small kindness you can do for your future self.

## Putting the Reset into Practice
If you’re just starting, don’t try to do all five of these at once. If you overhaul your entire Sunday routine, you’ll likely burn out by the second week. Start with just one—maybe the "Outfit Pre-Flight"—and see how it changes your Monday. Once that feels like a natural part of your routine, add the next one.

The goal isn't to be perfect or to have a flawlessly productive Sunday. The goal is to clear the path so that when Monday arrives, you’re starting from a place of readiness rather than a place of repair. Once you have your routine anchored, you can look for ways to optimize your setup even further.
