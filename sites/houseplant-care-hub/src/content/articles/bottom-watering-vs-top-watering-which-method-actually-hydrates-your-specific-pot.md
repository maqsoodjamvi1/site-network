If you’ve spent any time in plant forums, you’ve likely seen the debate: should you pour water from the top, or let the plant sit in a bowl to soak it up from the bottom? 

After killing my fair share of Calatheas and succulents by choosing the wrong method for the wrong potting mix, I’ve learned that this isn't just a matter of preference. It is a matter of physics. Your potting mix dictates how water moves, and choosing the wrong watering style is the fastest way to invite root rot or create a "dry pocket" that keeps your plant perpetually thirsty.

## The Problem with Top Watering and Hydrophobic Soil
Top watering is the default for most of us. You grab your watering can, pour, and wait for the runoff. It’s intuitive, but it has one major weakness: gravity. 

When you pour water over the top, it follows the path of least resistance. If your potting mix has become dry and shrunk away from the sides of the pot, the water will simply zip down those gaps and run straight out the drainage holes. The center of your root ball remains bone dry. 

Furthermore, many peat-based or coco-coir mixes become hydrophobic once they dry out. If you’ve ever poured a cup of water into a dry pot only to see it sit like a puddle on the surface for ten minutes, you’ve experienced this. The water refuses to saturate the soil because the fibers are essentially water-repellant. 

**Use top watering if:**
* You are using a very chunky, airy mix (like bark and perlite for orchids or Anthuriums).
* You want to flush out mineral salts or fertilizer buildup.
* You are dealing with a plant that has delicate surface roots you want to encourage.

## Why Bottom Watering Isn't Always the Answer
Bottom watering works by capillary action. You place your pot in a tray or sink with an inch or two of water. The soil wicks the moisture upward, ensuring the entire root ball is saturated. It’s excellent for preventing fungus gnats, as the top layer of soil stays dry, giving those pests no moist surface to lay eggs in.

However, bottom watering has a trap. If you leave a pot in water for too long—say, overnight—the soil becomes completely saturated. If your mix is too dense, like standard garden soil or a potting mix with too much peat, it can hold onto that water for weeks. This is a death sentence for plants like Snake Plants or ZZ plants that need their soil to dry out between waterings. 

**Bottom watering works best for:**
* African Violets, which hate getting their hairy leaves wet.
* Plants in smaller pots where the soil wicks evenly.
* Plants with dense root systems that have outgrown the pot and are hard to penetrate from the top.

## How to Test Your Mix Before You Choose
Before you decide on a method, you need to know what you’re working with. Take a handful of your potting mix—is it gritty and loose, or is it dark, dense, and "muddy" when wet?

If your mix is heavy (lots of compost or peat), avoid bottom watering unless you are extremely disciplined about setting a timer. If you leave a heavy mix in a bowl of water for an hour, you are likely suffocating the roots. 

If your mix is light and airy (lots of perlite, chunky bark, and pumice), top watering might leave large dry spots. In this case, bottom watering is superior because it forces the water into the nooks and crannies of the chunky bark that would otherwise be missed by a quick top-down pour.

## A Practical Step-by-Step for "Trouble" Plants
If you have a plant that looks like it’s drooping despite the soil looking moist on top, it’s likely suffering from the "dry core" issue mentioned earlier. Here is how to fix it:

1. **Check the weight:** Pick the pot up. If it feels suspiciously light, your soil has shrunk away from the sides and the roots are dry.
2. **The "Immersion" method:** Instead of just putting it in a saucer, fill a container with room-temperature water. Submerge the pot only halfway up its height. 
3. **The 20-minute rule:** Let it sit for 20 minutes. Check the top of the soil. If it’s still dry, leave it for another 10 minutes.
4. **The final drain:** Once the surface feels damp, remove the pot and let it drain in the sink for at least 15 minutes. Never put a pot back on its saucer while it is still dripping.

## Choosing Your Method for Long-Term Success
I generally stick to a hybrid approach. I top-water my chunky-mix aroids once a month to ensure the fertilizer salts are pushed out the bottom, and I use bottom-soaking for my smaller, peat-heavy plants to ensure the roots get a deep drink without drowning the top inch of soil.

If you are just starting out, prioritize the drainage. Regardless of whether you top or bottom water, the most important factor is the pot itself. Ensure you have drainage holes, and if your soil is taking more than a week to dry out, you need to add more perlite or pumice to your mix next time you repot.
