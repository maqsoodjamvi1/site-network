I used to say "I’m sorry" so often that it became a verbal tic. If I bumped into a chair, I apologized to the furniture. If someone interrupted me while I was speaking, I apologized for being in the way of their interruption. 

For a long time, I thought this was just politeness. I told myself I was being considerate, keeping the peace, and being "low maintenance." But eventually, I realized that my constant apologizing wasn't about being kind; it was a preemptive strike against rejection. I was apologizing for existing in a way that might inconvenience someone else, just in case they were already annoyed with me.

If you find yourself doing the same, you aren't just being polite. You are likely signaling to yourself—and to everyone around you—that you don't feel entitled to your own space or opinions.

## The Difference Between Accountability and Defensiveness

We need to distinguish between a functional apology and a reflexive one. A functional apology happens when you actually cause harm or break a social contract. If you show up twenty minutes late to a meeting, "I’m sorry I’m late" is a factual acknowledgment of a mistake. 

Chronic over-apologizing, however, sounds like this:
* "Sorry to bother you, but do you have a second?"
* "Sorry, I think that might be wrong, but..."
* "Sorry, I’m just going to squeeze past you."

In these scenarios, you aren't apologizing for a moral error; you are apologizing for occupying space. When you lead your requests with "sorry," you are implicitly suggesting that your request is an imposition or a burden. You are training the person you’re speaking to that your needs are secondary to theirs.

## Why We Do It: The "Shield" Mentality

Why do we do this? It usually stems from a subconscious belief that if we keep ourselves small, we are safer. If I apologize before you can criticize me, I have technically taken the "blame" off the table. It is a way to manage other people’s emotions before they even have a chance to manifest.

If you grew up in an environment where your needs were treated as inconveniences, or if you were frequently blamed for things outside of your control, you likely learned that the best way to avoid conflict was to be the first one to say, "My bad." 

This creates a cycle of low self-esteem. Every time you apologize for something you didn't do wrong, you are reinforcing a message to your own brain: *I am someone who causes problems. I am someone who needs to minimize myself to be accepted.*

## Practical Ways to Stop the Reflex

Breaking this habit isn't about becoming rude or arrogant; it's about shifting from apologetic language to appreciative language. Here are three practical tactics I used to change my own behavior:

* **Replace "Sorry" with "Thank You":** This is the single most effective tool. When you are late, instead of "Sorry I’m late," try, "Thank you for waiting for me." When you ask a colleague for help, instead of "Sorry to bug you," try, "I appreciate you taking the time to look at this." It shifts the tone from a place of shame to a place of gratitude.
* **The Pause Technique:** Before you speak, force a three-second silence. Most reflexive apologies happen in the first half-second of a sentence. That delay gives your brain enough time to realize that you aren't actually sorry—you’re just asking a question or stating a fact.
* **Audit Your "Sorry" Budget:** For one full day, keep a tally of how many times you say "sorry." Don't try to stop, just count. Seeing the number—usually between 15 and 30 times for chronic offenders—is often enough to make you consciously catch yourself the next day.

## Dealing with the "I'm Too Much" Narrative

Many people fear that if they stop apologizing, they will come across as entitled or aggressive. This is a common cognitive distortion. People generally don't think you are "entitled" because you stopped apologizing for existing; they usually respect you more because you communicate with clarity.

When you remove the "sorry," you leave room for the actual point of your message. If you are asking for a raise, a project extension, or just a quiet evening to yourself, the request is stronger when it stands on its own. 

## Moving Forward

It takes time to rewire the habit of self-diminishment. You will slip up, and that’s okay. The goal isn't to be perfect, but to be intentional. Each time you withhold a useless apology, you are essentially telling yourself that your presence is valid and your needs are reasonable.

If you’re ready to start building that sense of self-respect, the next step is to choose one interaction today where you would normally apologize and try the gratitude pivot instead. Once you see that the world doesn't end when you stop shrinking yourself, you’ll find it much easier to hold your ground in more difficult conversations.
