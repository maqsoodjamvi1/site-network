If you are currently navigating a high-conflict co-parenting relationship, your inbox is likely a source of daily dread. You know the feeling: a notification pops up, and your heart rate spikes because you recognize the sender’s address. You open the email to find a wall of text filled with accusations, passive-aggressive jabs, or demands that ignore the reality of your shared schedule.

In the heat of the moment, the human instinct is to hit "Reply" and set the record straight. You want to defend your parenting, point out their hypocrisy, or correct the factual errors. But in a high-conflict dynamic, this is exactly the fuel the other person is looking for. Every defensive paragraph you write is an opportunity for them to escalate the argument further.

I learned the hard way that you cannot control how your ex writes, but you can control how you respond. The BIFF method is a tool for stripping the emotion out of your correspondence so you can focus entirely on the logistical facts of raising your children.

## What is the BIFF Method?

BIFF is an acronym that stands for **Brief, Informative, Friendly, and Firm**. It is not a tool designed to win an argument or prove you are the "better" parent. It is a communication strategy designed to end the conversation as quickly as possible.

*   **Brief:** Keep your response to three sentences or fewer whenever possible. If you are replying to a paragraph of insults, you do not need to address every single lie. Address the logistical question and move on.
*   **Informative:** Stick to the facts. If the email is about a doctor’s appointment, state the time and location. If it is about a school pickup, state who is doing it. Do not include your opinion on why they are late or why their suggestion is inconvenient.
*   **Friendly:** This does not mean you have to be warm or affectionate. It means using a neutral, polite tone. Think of how you would speak to a coworker you don’t particularly like but must remain professional with. Use phrases like, "Thank you for the update," or "I appreciate the heads-up on the schedule change."
*   **Firm:** This is the most important part. A firm response leaves no room for further negotiation or back-and-forth. It closes the door on the topic so the conversation doesn't spiral into a three-day email chain.

## Putting BIFF Into Practice: A Real-World Scenario

Let’s say you receive an email from your ex that says: *"You’re always late for the Tuesday pickup, and it’s completely ruining the kids' routine. You clearly don't care about their extracurriculars, and I’m tired of having to fix your messes. Be there by 4:00 sharp or don’t bother showing up."*

A typical, reactive response might look like this: *"That’s not true. I was late once last month because of an accident on the highway, and you’re the one who is late all the time. You’re just trying to make me look bad in front of the kids."*

This response is a trap. It invites them to send a longer email explaining why you are actually the one at fault. 

**Using the BIFF method, your response should look like this:**

*"Thank you for the heads-up. I have the Tuesday pickup scheduled for 4:00 PM and will be there on time. See you then."*

Notice how this ignores the insults entirely. By not engaging with the accusation that you "don't care," you deny them the fight they are looking for. You have acknowledged the logistical need (the time) and remained professional.

## Why This Works Over Time

When you stop reacting to the bait, the pattern of communication eventually shifts. If you consistently respond with boring, neutral, and factual emails, the "reward" for your ex’s hostile communication disappears. They are no longer getting the emotional reaction or the debate they were seeking.

However, be prepared for an "extinction burst." When you first start using BIFF, your ex may escalate their hostility because their usual tactics are no longer working. They might send more emails, become more aggressive, or accuse you of being "cold" or "robotic." Stay the course. Keep your responses short, factual, and neutral. Do not justify why you are changing your communication style; simply continue to exhibit the new, calm behavior.

## When to Stop Replying Altogether

There are times when the BIFF method isn't enough, especially if the emails have devolved into pure harassment, threats, or name-calling. In these cases, you are under no obligation to reply to everything. 

If an email contains no logistical information—if it is just a vent or a personal attack—you can choose not to respond at all. If you feel you must respond to protect yourself legally, a simple, "I am not going to engage in this type of conversation. Please limit our communication to topics regarding the children's schedule and well-being," is enough.

Mastering this method takes practice, and you will likely slip up and write a long, defensive email a few times. When that happens, don't beat yourself up. Just delete the draft, take a walk, and try again with the next message. The goal isn't to be perfect; the goal is to protect your peace and ensure that your limited energy goes toward your children, not toward winning an inbox war.

Once you have established a pattern of neutral, factual communication, you may find that the volume of hostile emails naturally decreases.
