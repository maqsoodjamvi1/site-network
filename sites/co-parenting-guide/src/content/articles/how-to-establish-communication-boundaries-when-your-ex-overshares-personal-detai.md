When I first started co-parenting, I made the mistake of thinking that "co-parenting" meant "keeping the lines of communication wide open." My ex would text me about everything: their dating life, their frustrations with their job, or even their opinion on my personal life. I felt obligated to listen because we shared kids, and I worried that being distant would hurt our cooperation.

I was wrong. Oversharing isn't collaboration; it’s an emotional entanglement that burns you out and makes it harder to focus on what actually matters: your children. If your ex uses your text threads as a diary or a sounding board, you aren’t obligated to play the role of confidant. Here is how I set boundaries that stuck, and how you can do the same.

## Move to a Specialized Communication App

The biggest mistake I made was texting via my personal phone number. It made every alert feel like an emergency. When you use your personal SMS, you’re always "on call."

Switching to a co-parenting app like OurFamilyWizard, TalkingParents, or AppClose was the single most effective boundary I set. These apps create a physical and digital wall between "parenting business" and "personal life." When you receive a notification from an app, your brain recognizes it as a work task, not a personal text from a friend or family member.

If your ex insists on using text or email, keep your responses on these platforms strictly to the topic at hand. If they send a paragraph about why they’re upset with their boss, you simply do not acknowledge it. You don’t need to be rude; you just need to be a "grey rock." Respond only to the logistical parts of the message. If the message is 100% personal, don't reply at all.

## The "Pivot and Return" Technique

When you are in person or on a phone call, it is much harder to ignore an overshare. If your ex starts telling you about their new partner or their family drama, don't sit there silently and don't engage with questions. Use the "Pivot and Return" method.

The goal is to acknowledge the person is speaking, pivot to the child, and return the conversation to the schedule.

*   **The Ex:** "I just don't know why my sister thinks she can criticize my parenting, it’s like she forgot I’m the one doing all the work..."
*   **You (The Pivot):** "I hear that you're frustrated with your family situation."
*   **You (The Return):** "I’d like to keep our conversation focused on the kids. Does Saturday still work for the drop-off at 4:00 PM?"

By validating the emotion with one sentence, you acknowledge you heard them, but by immediately pivoting to the schedule, you signal that this is not a space for an in-depth emotional conversation.

## Define Your "Business Hours"

I used to answer texts at 10:00 PM because I wanted to be "available." All this did was encourage late-night venting sessions. You are not a 24/7 service.

Decide on a timeframe when you are available for non-emergency communication—for example, 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM on weekdays. If your ex messages you at 9:30 PM about a non-urgent topic, do not open the message until the next morning. If they start a rant, send a scripted, pre-written response:

*   "I am off the clock for the evening. If this is an emergency regarding the children’s health or safety, please call me. Otherwise, I will respond to this tomorrow during business hours."

## What to Include in Your "No-Go" List

You need to know exactly where you are drawing the line. It helps to write this down for yourself so you don't waver when you're caught off guard. I refuse to engage with:

*   **Complaints about their personal life:** Dating, friends, or work drama.
*   **Criticism of your choices:** Anything unrelated to the children’s immediate well-being.
*   **Requests for validation:** "Don't you think I'm being reasonable here?" (My answer is always, "I'm not the right person to discuss this with.")
*   **Reminiscing:** Stories about "the way things used to be" or "remember when we..."

## Protecting Your Peace

When you set these boundaries, expect pushback. Your ex might call you "cold," "unhelpful," or "difficult." This is a natural reaction when someone loses an outlet they’ve been using for emotional regulation. Stay the course. Remind yourself that by refusing to be their therapist or confidant, you are protecting your own energy so you can be a more present, patient parent when you are with your kids.

You don't need to explain why you're changing your behavior. You don't need their permission to stop listening to their personal drama. You are simply shifting your dynamic from "confidants who used to be married" to "business partners raising children." It is a professional transition, and it’s one that will eventually make both of your lives—and the lives of your kids—much less chaotic.

Taking these steps requires a bit of grit, especially in the first few weeks. If you’re feeling overwhelmed by the transition or aren't sure how to handle a specific recurring conversation, it can be helpful to look at resources that outline deeper strategies for high-conflict communication.
