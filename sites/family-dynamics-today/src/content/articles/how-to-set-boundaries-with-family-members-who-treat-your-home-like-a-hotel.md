If you have a spare bedroom or even just a decent pull-out couch, you probably know the drill. A relative texts you on a Tuesday saying they are "passing through town next month," and before you even reply, they have already assumed they are staying with you for five days. 

Soon, your kitchen is full of strangers to your normal routine, your grocery bill spikes, and you are playing unpaid concierge in your own house. Family members treating your home like a hotel is a uniquely exhausting brand of frustration because you want to be welcoming, but you also want your living room back. 

Setting boundaries here requires shifting away from passive hints—like sighing loudly while changing the guest sheets—and moving toward clear, polite, but firm communication. Here is how I finally got my own family to respect my space, without blowing up holiday dinners in the process.

## Define Your Non-Negotiables Before They Book

The biggest mistake I used to make was waiting until my guests arrived to figure out what was bothering me. By the time they were sleeping on my air mattress, I was already resentful of the noise, the mess, and the lack of privacy. 

Before anyone comes to stay next, sit down by yourself and map out your hard limits. These are not up for debate. They might include:
* A strict maximum of three nights for any casual visit.
* No staying over during your busiest work weeks of the month.
* Requiring at least two weeks of advance notice so you can block off the time.
* Setting a rule that you do not provide transportation to and from an airport that is an hour away.

When you know your exact capacity, you stop guessing what is "fair" in the heat of the moment and start operating from a clear checklist. 

## Use the "Pillow-Soft, Steel-Hard" Script

When a relative casually mentions visiting, people-pleasers tend to waffle. We say things like, "Well, let me check my schedule, it might be a bit crazy then," which relatives translate to, "Keep begging, I'll probably say yes."

You need a script that sounds warm on the surface but holds a brick wall underneath. Instead of apologizing, use the sandwich method: validate the relationship, state the logistical reality, and offer an alternative if applicable.

Try saying something like this: "It is so great that you're coming to the city! We love seeing you, but our house is at full capacity this month and we just can't host overnight guests right now. Let's definitely grab dinner together on Thursday night, though—I can send you a list of a few nice boutique hotels nearby, too."

Notice what this does: it expresses love, shuts down the lodging immediately, and immediately pivots to a manageable interaction. If they push back by saying, "Oh, we don't mind sleeping on the floor," your reply does not change. You simply repeat the logistical boundary: "I know you wouldn't mind, but our house rule right now is no overnight guests. Let's look at those hotels."

## Stop Acting Like Hotel Staff

If family members treat your home like a hotel, it is often because you are accidentally playing the role of the concierge. When guests arrive and you immediately ask what they want for breakfast, offer to do their laundry, and map out their daily tourist itinerary, you are setting an unsustainable standard.

Guests need to see that you live a real, busy life that doesn't pause just because they brought a suitcase. The next time family stays over, establish the rhythm of the house immediately:
* Point out where the coffee maker is and tell them they are free to brew their own in the morning.
* Leave a basket of clean towels out, show them the laundry hamper, and let them know they are responsible for their own towels after day two.
* Keep your own schedule. If you normally go to the gym at 7 AM on Saturday, do not cancel it to sit and wait for them to wake up. Leave a spare key and tell them to lock up behind themselves.

When hosting requires them to be self-sufficient, the relatives who just want a free bed with room service tend to start looking into Airbnb options for their next trip. 

## Handle the "Infinite Stay" Trap

The hardest scenario to navigate is the relative who arrives for a weekend and, by Sunday night, has somehow made zero mention of leaving. They start unpacking their second suitcase into your dresser drawers, and panic sets in.

You have to reset the timeline immediately. Do not wait until the end of the week hoping they will check the calendar. Pull them aside on Monday morning and get explicit about the departure date. 

You can frame it around your upcoming schedule: "Hey, just so we're on the same page, what time are you heading out on Wednesday? I have a massive project deadline starting Thursday morning and I need to get the guest room cleared out to use as a temporary workspace." 

This removes the emotion from the request. It isn't that you are sick of them (even if you are); it is that your physical space has a strict operational requirement.

Protecting your home environment doesn't make you a bad relative; it makes you a functional adult with finite energy and square footage. By taking control of the calendar, dropping the hotel-staff mindset, and holding your ground with calm scripts, you can reclaim your sanctuary and maybe even enjoy visits from your family again.
