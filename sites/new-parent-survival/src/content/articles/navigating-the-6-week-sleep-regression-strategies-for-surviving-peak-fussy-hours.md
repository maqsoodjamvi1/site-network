By the time your baby hits the six-week mark, you have likely just started to find a rhythm. Maybe you finally figured out how to get the swaddle tight, or you’ve identified the difference between a "hungry" cry and a "tired" cry. Then, seemingly overnight, the wheels come off. The baby who was sleeping three-hour stretches is suddenly waking every 45 minutes, and the evening "witching hour" feels more like an endurance test than a routine.

This is the six-week sleep regression. It isn’t a sign that you’re doing something wrong; it is a sign that your baby’s brain is undergoing a massive developmental leap. They are becoming more alert, their circadian rhythms are beginning to shift, and they are suddenly aware of the world in a way they weren't at two weeks old.

## Why Six Weeks Feels Different

At six weeks, a baby’s eyes track movement more consistently, and they are beginning to take in sensory input rather than just reacting to physical hunger or discomfort. This "waking up" to the world makes it harder for them to settle down. When they get overstimulated during the day, their cortisol levels spike. By the time 6:00 PM rolls around, they are overtired, which makes the act of falling asleep feel impossible for them.

If you are currently in the thick of this, your goal isn't to "sleep train"—it is too early for that. Your goal is survival and sensory management.

## Practical Tactics for the Evening Peak

The "witching hour" usually occurs between 5:00 PM and 9:00 PM. During this window, your baby is likely to be frantic and difficult to soothe. Here is how I handled this phase with my own kids:

*   **The "Low-Stim" Transition:** Starting at 4:30 PM, dim the lights in your living area. Turn off the television and keep your voice low. You are signaling to the baby that the day is winding down, even if they aren't ready to sleep yet.
*   **Motion over Stillness:** If the baby is screaming despite being fed and changed, stop trying to rock them in a stationary chair. Move. Put them in a carrier and walk around the kitchen, or go for a walk outside if the weather allows. The rhythmic motion of your body acts as a reset button for their nervous system.
*   **The "Reset" Bath:** A warm bath can break the cycle of crying. If they are mid-meltdown, strip them down for a quick, warm water session. The change in temperature and the tactile sensation of water often distracts them enough to break the crying loop.
*   **White Noise Volume:** Don't be afraid to turn the white noise up. A baby’s perspective on sound is different from ours; they find "loud" white noise comforting because it mimics the rushing sound of blood flow in the womb. If you use a machine, ensure it is set to a constant, flat tone rather than "rain" or "crickets," which have peaks and valleys that can startle a light sleeper.

## Protecting Your Own Mental Bandwidth

When you are sleep-deprived, the baby's crying can feel like a physical assault. It is easy to spiral into thoughts like, "I will never sleep again," or "Why is this baby so unhappy?" 

It is vital to create a tagging system with your partner or a support person. If you are the one who has been on "baby duty" since 5:00 AM, you are likely depleted by the time the evening regression hits. If possible, agree on a hard shift change. At 7:00 PM, hand the baby to your partner, put on noise-canceling headphones, and go into another room for 30 minutes. You don't even have to sleep—just listening to a podcast or staring at a wall in silence can prevent that "fight or flight" response from taking over your parenting.

## Managing the Nighttime Wake-Ups

During this regression, the baby will likely wake up more frequently. The biggest mistake I made during the first few weeks was turning on the lights or engaging in "play" during these night wakings. Even if they are awake and looking at you, keep the interaction transaction-based.

Keep the lights off—or use a very dim red-light lamp if you need to see—and keep the room temperature slightly cool. If they wake up and start "cooing" or looking around, resist the urge to smile back or chatter. If they aren't crying, wait a few minutes before intervening. Sometimes a baby will wake up, make a few noises, and drift back to sleep on their own. If you pick them up the second they stir, you might be interrupting their process of learning how to settle.

## Focus on the Daytime "Wake Windows"

While the night is for sleep, the day is for sensory input. To keep the baby from being a wreck at night, you need to ensure they aren't "napping" for too long during the day. At six weeks, most babies can only stay awake for about 60 to 75 minutes at a time. 

If they stay awake for two hours, they will be overtired. If they sleep for four hours straight during the day, they will likely be up all night. Try to keep them on a rhythm where they eat, have a short period of play (tummy time or looking at a high-contrast card), and then go back down for a nap shortly after the one-hour mark.

This phase is intense, but it is temporary. By keeping the environment calm and managing your own fatigue, you can bridge the gap until they settle into a more predictable cycle. These next few weeks are really just about keeping everyone fed, dry, and sane until the baby’s internal clock catches up to the world.
