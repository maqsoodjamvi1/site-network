If you find yourself constantly checking your phone to see if you’ve sent the last three messages in a group chat, or if you feel like the designated “event planner” of your friend group, you aren’t imagining things. There is a specific kind of burnout that comes with being the bridge between people. It’s what I call "Initiator Fatigue."

You reach a point where you start to wonder: *If I stopped planning, would we ever actually see each other?*

This isn't about keeping score in a petty way. It’s about energy management. If you are the only person fueling the connection, the friendship becomes a job rather than a source of support. Here is how to audit your social life and figure out if you need to adjust your approach.

## The Three-Cycle Audit

To determine if you are carrying an uneven load, look back at the last three times you hung out with a specific friend or group. Don't look at the quality of the time spent—look at the logistics of how it happened. 

Ask yourself these three questions:
1. Did I suggest the date, time, and location?
2. Did I follow up to confirm or send the calendar invite?
3. If I hadn't reached out, would there have been a "Plan B" or an alternative suggestion from them?

If the answer to all three is "Yes," you are likely in a one-sided scheduling dynamic. We often tell ourselves that our friends are "just busy" or "not planners." While that might be true, it doesn't change the fact that you are the one bearing the mental load. If you are the only one doing the emotional labor of maintaining the relationship, it is reasonable to feel frustrated.

## The "Step-Back" Experiment

The only way to know if your friend is incapable of planning or just reliant on your initiative is to conduct an experiment. I call this the "Step-Back."

For the next month, stop being the one to reach out first. Do not send the "thinking of you" texts, do not suggest dinner, and do not organize the group birthday outing. 

This isn't a game to punish them; it’s a reset. If you are constantly filling the silence, the other person never has the opportunity to miss your company or realize that the responsibility has shifted entirely onto you. 

*   **Scenario A:** They reach out within a week or two to grab coffee. This confirms they value the friendship and are capable of planning; they just got used to you doing it.
*   **Scenario B:** Silence. The month goes by, and you don’t hear from them. This is the hard part. It’s not necessarily a sign that they hate you, but it is a data point. It confirms that the friendship is currently only sustained by your specific effort.

## What to Do When the Silence Stretches

If you hit the end of your experiment and realize you’ve heard nothing, don’t jump to conclusions about your worth. Instead, use that clarity to make a decision about how you want to invest your time moving forward.

You have three practical choices when you realize you’re the sole initiator:

*   **The "Low-Stakes" Shift:** Change how you view the friendship. Stop treating them as a "core" friend who requires your weekly planning energy. Treat them as a "casual" friend. If you happen to be in their neighborhood, you can shoot them a text, but don’t schedule your life around them.
*   **The Direct Conversation:** Sometimes, a friend has no idea they are off-loading all the work onto you. You can say: "I’ve realized I’ve been the one coordinating our plans for a while, and I’m feeling a bit burnt out on the planning side. I’d love to see you more, but I need you to lead the planning for our next couple of get-togethers."
*   **The Gradual Fade:** If the friendship only exists because you are the architect, it is okay to let that structure naturally dissolve. You are allowed to stop working on projects that don't return your investment.

## When to Let Go

There is a difference between a friend who is bad at logistics and a friend who is bad at prioritizing. A friend who is bad at logistics will eventually notice your absence and reach out in their own clumsy, late, or disorganized way. A friend who is simply not prioritizing you will not notice the silence at all.

Recognizing the difference is the key to protecting your own social battery. When you stop over-extending for people who aren't meeting you halfway, you create space for new connections that might actually be reciprocal.

It is normal to feel a bit of grief when you stop reaching out and realize the phone really doesn't ring. But remember: the effort you save by not chasing people who won't meet you halfway is energy you can pour into friends who make you feel seen, valued, and—most importantly—pursued in return.

Taking these steps requires a bit of courage, but the result is a social circle that is actually sustainable. Once you have assessed your current dynamic and decided which friendships need a shift, the next steps involve re-evaluating how you spend your free time and where you want to focus your social energy.
