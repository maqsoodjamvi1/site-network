We often measure the health of a friendship by how much fun we have, but there is a more reliable metric: the "Reciprocity Audit." This isn't about keeping a tally of who paid for the last round of drinks or who texted first. Instead, it is an assessment of whether your input, boundaries, and emotional labor are treated as valuable currency or as background noise.

If you feel like you are constantly auditioning for your friend’s attention or that your advice is a suggestion they only follow when it suits them, it’s time to look at the patterns.

## The "One-Way Mirror" Effect

The most common sign that your input isn’t valued is the "One-Way Mirror" dynamic. You know the scenario: You spend forty minutes listening to them vent about their workplace drama. You offer specific solutions—maybe suggesting they talk to HR or update their LinkedIn profile. They nod, agree, and then proceed to do the exact opposite or continue complaining about the same issue for three months.

When you then bring up a minor stressor in your own life, they check their phone, change the subject, or offer a hollow "That sucks, anyway did you see that new show?"

To audit this, look for the "Pivot Point." The next time you are sharing something important, monitor how quickly they pivot the conversation back to themselves. If they consistently hijack the narrative within two sentences of you opening up, they aren’t looking for a friend; they are looking for an audience.

## Testing the "Expertise Threshold"

We all have friends who ask for advice they have no intention of taking. That’s normal. But there is a difference between someone who is indecisive and someone who actively devalues your specific expertise.

Try this experiment: If you are an expert in a specific area—say, you’re an accountant or a talented home cook—give your friend advice on a topic they specifically asked you about. If they immediately counter your suggestion with, "Well, my cousin read on a forum that..." or "I don't think that would work for me," watch how they handle the information.

If they treat your specialized knowledge as a mere opinion to be debated rather than a contribution to be considered, you are being devalued. A friend who respects you will at least weigh your perspective against their own rather than dismissing it out of hand to preserve their own narrative.

## The "Low-Stakes Request" Filter

Sometimes, we convince ourselves that a friend values us because they show up for the "big" things—birthdays, weddings, or crises. But the real test of value happens in the low-stakes, inconvenient moments.

Conduct this audit using the "Favor Threshold":

*   **The Request:** Ask for a favor that is slightly inconvenient but reasonable. For example: "Hey, I’m trying to finish this project, could you look at this paragraph and tell me if it makes sense?" or "I’m going to be in your neighborhood for ten minutes, could I drop off this book I borrowed?"
*   **The Reaction:** A friend who values your input will either say yes, or, if they are busy, offer a specific alternative time. 
*   **The Red Flag:** If they ignore the request entirely, make a joke out of it to deflect, or agree but then consistently "forget" to follow through, they are demonstrating that their time is significantly more valuable than yours.

If you find yourself apologizing for asking for their time or attention, the imbalance has already become part of the relationship’s foundation.

## Breaking the Cycle of Over-Functioning

If you realize during this audit that you are the one doing all the heavy lifting, you don’t necessarily need to end the friendship tomorrow. Instead, you need to stop over-functioning.

Stop offering solutions to problems they aren't interested in solving. If they start a "venting session," simply say, "I’m not in a great headspace to process work stress today, but I’d love to talk about something lighter." 

Watch their reaction to this new boundary. A friend who values you will adjust their behavior because they want to keep you in their life. A friend who only valued you for your utility as an unpaid therapist or a sounding board will likely drift away or become agitated. Either way, you get your time and energy back.

Value isn't something you can demand; it is something that is either present or absent in the way someone treats your presence. By performing these small, deliberate audits, you stop guessing where you stand and start seeing the reality of the dynamic clearly. 

Once you have identified these patterns, the next step is to decide how you want to adjust your own availability to match the level of respect you are actually receiving.
