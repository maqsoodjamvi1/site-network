Long-distance relationships thrive on the promise of the future. When you are separated by time zones or state lines, the "closing plan"—the concrete strategy for moving into the same city—acts as the anchor that keeps the relationship from drifting into a pen-pal dynamic.

I’ve been there. I remember sitting in a dimly lit apartment, nursing a lukewarm coffee while watching my partner pixelate on a video call, realizing we were talking about "one day" instead of "next August." If you are starting to notice that your future feels more like a vague hope than a scheduled event, it might be time to take a hard look at the logistics.

Here are five signs that your long-distance relationship is currently lacking the framework needed to actually become a permanent one.

## 1. The "When" Has Been Replaced by "Eventually"
"Eventually" is the death knell for a long-distance relationship. When you first start dating long-distance, it’s normal to be vague because you are still vetting the connection. But if you have been together for more than six months and you still haven't discussed a specific window for closing the gap, you aren't planning; you are just waiting.

If you bring up a move or a timeline and your partner immediately pivots to phrases like "we'll see what happens with my job" or "it depends on how things go," they are likely keeping the relationship in a state of suspended animation. A concrete plan involves a target date, even if that date is two years away. If you don’t have a month and a year written on a calendar, you don't have a plan.

## 2. You’ve Stopped Talking About Relocation Logistics
A real closing plan involves boring, unromantic, and necessary logistical discussions. Have you looked at the cost of living in each other's cities? Have you had a serious conversation about who would actually move?

If you find that every time you bring up the practical side—like breaking a lease, transferring a job, or what to do with your furniture—your partner changes the subject or acts as if you are "ruining the mood," that is a red flag. Real commitment looks like researching apartment prices in their city or figuring out how you’d handle a cross-country move with your pets. If you are the only one doing the research, you are the only one currently planning the future.

## 3. Your Visits Are "Vacations," Not "Trial Runs"
Many long-distance couples fall into the trap of only seeing each other in high-intensity, short-duration bursts. You spend your weekends together going to concerts, eating at nice restaurants, or visiting tourist spots. While this is great for bonding, it is terrible for reality-testing.

If you have been dating for a year and you have never spent a full week together in a "normal" setting—grocery shopping, doing laundry, or just sitting in the same room while you both work—you lack a baseline for what living together will actually look like. If your partner resists the idea of "boring" time together in favor of keeping visits purely recreational, they might be addicted to the fantasy of the relationship rather than the reality of a shared life.

## 4. There Is No "Exit Strategy" for the Current Living Situation
Sometimes, we get stuck in a long-distance rut because one person has a career or family situation that they are unwilling to change, yet they refuse to admit it. Look at your partner’s current life. Are they building a professional or personal infrastructure that makes it impossible to leave?

If they have just signed a three-year lease, accepted a promotion that requires them to be on-site five days a week, or bought a house, and you haven't discussed how that impacts your timeline, you are ignoring the elephant in the room. A partner who wants a future with you will openly discuss how to work around these barriers. If the barriers are treated as "just the way it is," you need to ask yourself if you are willing to spend three more years waiting for a lease to expire.

## 5. You Are Avoiding the "Deal-Breaker" Conversations
Closing the distance is a massive life change. It usually involves one person making a significant sacrifice. If you haven't had the hard, uncomfortable conversations about how that sacrifice will be handled, you aren't ready to move.

Have you discussed what happens if the move goes poorly? What if one of you finds it impossible to adjust to the new city? What if you move for them and then you can’t find work in your field? If you feel like you can't ask these questions for fear of "scaring them off," the relationship is built on thin ice. A solid closing plan requires total transparency about expectations, including the "what-ifs."

### Assessing Your Next Moves

If you recognize these patterns, don't panic. Sometimes, a lack of a plan is just a result of a lack of clear communication, not a lack of affection. The goal isn't to force a move tomorrow, but to align your expectations so you aren't wasting years on a relationship that is fundamentally incompatible with your future goals.

Before you decide whether to stay, go, or push for a deeper conversation, it is helpful to organize your own thoughts and look at the objective reality of the commitment level you are receiving versus what you are giving. Identifying where the breakdown is happening is the first step toward deciding what you need to do next to protect your time and your heart.
