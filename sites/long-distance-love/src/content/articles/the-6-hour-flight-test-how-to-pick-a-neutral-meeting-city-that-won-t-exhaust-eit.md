Planning a trip to see someone new usually starts with a flurry of excitement followed immediately by the logistical headache of a map. If you live in Seattle and they live in Atlanta, a weekend visit suddenly turns into a cross-country endurance race involving red-eye flights, lost luggage, and Monday morning exhaustion. 

When you are trying to figure out if a relationship has long-term potential, the last thing you want is for the geography to do all the heavy lifting—or rather, the heavy draining. That is why I started using what I call the 6-Hour Flight Test. It is a practical method for picking a neutral meeting city that keeps the travel time fair, preserves your energy, and gives you both a clean slate to actually enjoy each other's company.

Here is how to set up the test, map out the logistics, and choose a city that supports the relationship instead of testing your patience.

## Cap Total Travel Time at Six Hours Door-to-Door

The biggest mistake people make when choosing a meeting spot is looking strictly at flight times on a map. A direct flight might only be three hours in the air, but once you add getting to the airport two hours early, parking, security, and a 45-minute rideshare to your hotel, that three-hour flight becomes a grueling seven-hour ordeal.

When applying the 6-Hour Flight Test, calculate the entire journey from your front door to the hotel lobby. If either of you has to endure more than six hours of transit, someone is going to arrive grumpy, stiff, and regretting their life choices. 

For example, if you are flying out of Chicago and your partner is coming from Denver, meeting somewhere in the middle like Kansas City or Omaha cuts everyone’s travel time way down. Instead of a full day wasted in transit terminals, you both arrive by early afternoon with enough energy to check into the hotel and grab dinner without needing a nap first.

## Pick a City with Direct Flights for Both of You

Connecting flights are relationship hazard zones. A single weather delay in a hub city like Dallas or Chicago can turn a straightforward weekend getaway into an overnight nightmare of sleeping on airport floor mats.

When you open up Google Flights or Skyscanner, look exclusively for cities that offer direct, non-stop routes from both of your home airports. If you live in Boston and your partner is in Austin, meeting in Nashville works well because both cities have direct flights there that take under three hours. 

If direct options are limited, prioritize the person who has the more demanding work schedule or is newer to long-distance logistics. Minimizing connections for at least one of you sets a baseline of ease for the weekend.

## Choose a Neutral Zone with an Easy Airport-to-City Transit

The point of a neutral meeting city is to level the playing field. Neither of you should feel like a tourist navigating unfamiliar territory while the other acts as an exhausted local tour guide. 

Look for cities where the airport is remarkably close to the downtown core or entertainment districts. Cities like Portland (Oregon), Phoenix, and Salt Lake City have transit systems or short cab rides that get you from the baggage claim to your hotel in under 20 minutes. 

Avoid cities where you immediately have to rent a car, deal with brutal highway traffic, or figure out a confusing commuter rail just to get to where you are staying. The faster you can drop your bags and feel settled, the better your mood will be when you finally hug.

## Factor in a "Buffer Day" and Set Expectations

Even the best-planned neutral city trips can feel intense. You are trying to bridge the gap between your normal daily routines and shared time together, often in a hotel room with thin walls. 

To make the trip successful, use a simple logistical checklist before you book anything:
- Agree on a budget beforehand so one person does not end up subsidizing an expensive boutique hotel or high-end dining scene they did not vote for.
- Pick a hotel with decent common spaces—like a lobby lounge or an outdoor courtyard—so you have somewhere to decompress if you need 30 minutes of quiet time apart.
- Build in low-pressure activities. Instead of booking museums and theater tickets, plan for a long walk in a public park or sitting in a coffee shop. 

When you remove the logistical friction of travel, you give yourselves the mental space to focus on what actually matters: seeing how you naturally fit into each other's lives outside of text messages and video calls.

As you start looking at flight schedules and comparing potential meeting spots for your next trip, take a moment to look at how the numbers actually add up. Finding the right middle ground is often the first real test of how well you two can plan, compromise, and travel as a team.
