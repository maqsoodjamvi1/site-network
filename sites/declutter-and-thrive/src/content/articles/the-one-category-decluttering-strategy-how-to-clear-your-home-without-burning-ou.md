I spent years trying to organize my home by room. I would start in the kitchen on a Saturday morning, energized by a podcast, only to find myself three hours deep into a junk drawer, surrounded by half-empty batteries and expired takeout menus, while the rest of the room remained a disaster. By noon, I was exhausted, the kitchen looked worse than when I started, and I inevitably quit.

The "one-room" approach is a trap because our stuff rarely stays where it belongs. When you clean by room, you are constantly interrupted by items that belong elsewhere. You find a pair of scissors in the bathroom, a toy in the kitchen, and a stack of mail in the bedroom. You end up walking back and forth, losing focus, and eventually giving up.

The "one-category" strategy changed my home because it ignores geography. Instead of focusing on a space, you focus on a specific type of item, gathering every single instance of it from every corner of the house.

## Why Categories Win Over Rooms

When you commit to a category—for example, "all writing pens" or "all coffee mugs"—you are forced to confront the actual volume of what you own. You cannot hide the fact that you have forty-two pens if they are all sitting in a pile on your dining table. 

This method creates a clear "before and after" that doesn't rely on the layout of your house. It is also inherently faster because you aren't constantly leaving your workspace to put things away. You keep the items in front of you until the decision-making is done.

## How to Get Started (The Step-by-Step)

If you have a free hour, do not try to "declutter the living room." Pick one of these categories instead. Here is exactly how I execute this:

1.  **The Roundup:** Set a timer for 10 minutes. Go through every room—yes, every room—and pull out every item that falls into your chosen category. If you are doing mugs, check the kitchen cupboards, the dishwasher, the desk, the nightstand, and the garage shelf. Bring them all to one central surface, like your kitchen island or a cleared-off table.
2.  **The "Keep" Filter:** Do not start by throwing things away. Start by picking your absolute favorites. If you have 30 mugs but you only ever reach for the same three, set those three aside. These are your non-negotiables.
3.  **The Reality Check:** Look at the pile of remainders. Ask yourself, "If I didn't own this, would I buy it today?" If the answer is no, it goes into a donate box. If it is broken or missing pieces, put it in the trash.
4.  **The Final Home:** Now, put your "keep" pile back into its designated home. Because you have purged the excess, you will likely find that your storage space suddenly feels spacious rather than cramped.

## What to Tackle First (And What to Avoid)

Don’t start with sentimental items like old photos or children's artwork. Those require emotional energy that leads to burnout. Start with "neutral" categories where you feel no personal attachment.

*   **Easy wins:** Bed linens, towels, pens/pencils, mugs, socks, or cleaning supplies.
*   **Harder tasks:** Books, hobby equipment, or cold-weather clothing.
*   **The "Wait" list:** Anything that feels like it has a story attached to it. Leave those for when you have built up the "decluttering muscle."

## The "One-Box" Rule for Success

To keep the momentum going, I keep a dedicated "Donate" box in the bottom of my hallway closet at all times. When I finish a category, any item that didn't make the cut goes directly into that box. 

The biggest mistake people make is moving decluttered items to a "maybe" pile in the basement. That isn't decluttering; it’s just moving your mess to a different room. If you aren't sure about an item, put it back in your regular rotation for two weeks. If you haven't used it by then, you have your answer. 

## Maintaining the Gains

Once you have cleared a category, the maintenance is simple. Because you’ve already defined where those items belong, "putting things away" becomes a two-second task rather than a major project. For example, since I gathered all my batteries into one container, I no longer buy new ones when I can't find the old ones. I go to the "battery home," see what I have, and that’s it.

The goal isn't to have a showroom-perfect house; the goal is to stop spending your weekends managing your inventory. By shifting your focus from the walls of a room to the contents of a drawer, you can actually see progress in under an hour.
