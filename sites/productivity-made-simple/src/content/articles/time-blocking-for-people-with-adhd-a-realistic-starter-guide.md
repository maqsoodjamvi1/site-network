When I first tried time blocking, I treated my calendar like a rigid prison cell. I mapped out every fifteen-minute increment from 8:00 AM to 6:00 PM. By 10:30 AM, I was behind schedule, felt like a failure, and spent the rest of the day doom-scrolling because I had already "ruined" the plan.

If you have ADHD, the traditional advice—"just put it on your calendar"—is usually a recipe for frustration. Our brains often struggle with time blindness, meaning we underestimate how long tasks take and overestimate our ability to switch gears. 

Time blocking isn’t about forcing your brain to act like a machine. It’s about building a container that makes it easier for you to see what you’re actually capable of doing in a single day. Here is how I make it work without the crushing guilt.

## Start with "Anchor" Tasks
Don’t try to block your entire day. If you have an empty calendar, filling it with 20 tiny tasks will backfire. Instead, start by blocking your non-negotiables. These are the things that have a fixed time, like a 2:00 PM team meeting or picking up kids from school.

Once those are down, add your "energy-heavy" tasks. If you know you’re sharpest at 9:00 AM, block that hour for your most difficult work. If you know you hit a slump at 3:00 PM, don't schedule deep focus work then. Use that block for low-stakes tasks, like answering emails or filing expenses.

## The 50% Rule for Time Estimation
Most of us are terrible at estimating how long a task takes. We think, "I can write this report in an hour," when in reality, it takes ninety minutes of writing plus twenty minutes of formatting and ten minutes of staring at the ceiling.

To combat this, take your honest estimate and multiply it by 1.5. If you think a task will take an hour, block 90 minutes. This creates a "buffer zone" in your schedule. If you finish early, you get a guilt-free break. If you finish on time, you aren't perpetually behind.

## Build "Transition Blocks" Between Tasks
The most common mistake I see is back-to-back blocks. If you jump from a creative writing block straight into a budget spreadsheet, your brain will likely stall. This is called "task switching friction."

You need to build in formal transition time. I use 15-minute blocks between major tasks that are explicitly labeled as "Reset." During these 15 minutes, I am not allowed to work. I have to stand up, get water, stretch, or just walk to the kitchen. This signals to my brain that the previous task is closed and we are moving on to the next chapter.

## Use Color-Coding to Visualize Your Load
I use a digital calendar (Google Calendar or Outlook) and assign colors to categories. This helps me spot "over-blocking" at a glance. 

*   **Deep Work (Blue):** Tasks requiring high focus.
*   **Admin/Low Energy (Yellow):** Emails, phone calls, paperwork.
*   **Transitions (Grey):** My buffer zones and breaks.
*   **Fixed Events (Red):** Meetings and appointments.

If my week looks like a sea of red and blue with no yellow or grey, I know I’m heading toward a burnout cliff. If I see a day that is solid blue, I’ll intentionally move one task to the next day to balance the load.

## How to Handle the Inevitable "Off-Track" Moments
You will have days where you ignore your calendar entirely. That is not a failure of the system; it is a reality of human existence. When you realize you’ve fallen off track, don't try to "catch up" by jamming everything into the remaining hours. 

Instead, perform a "Calendar Triage." Look at your remaining blocks and pick the one most important task that absolutely must happen today. Delete or move the rest. Clearing the calendar feels better than staring at a list of things you know you won't finish. 

## A Simple Daily Workflow
If you want to try this tomorrow, here is the exact process:

*   **The Night Before:** Write down the 3 big things you want to finish. 
*   **The Morning Map:** Before you open your inbox, assign those 3 things to blocks on your calendar. Add 15 minutes of "transition" time between each.
*   **The Timer Method:** When you start a block, set a physical kitchen timer or a visual countdown app for the duration of that block. This helps mitigate time blindness by making the passage of time visible.
*   **The Review:** At the end of the day, look at what you actually finished versus what you planned. Did you need more buffer time? Was a task harder than you thought? Adjust tomorrow’s blocks accordingly.

Time blocking is meant to serve your goals, not satisfy an imaginary version of yourself who never gets distracted. The goal isn't a perfect calendar; it's a calendar that is accurate enough to help you make decisions about what to do next.

Once you have your core blocks set up, the next challenge is managing the distractions that inevitably pop up during your deep work sessions. To help you stay in the flow once you’ve cleared the space for it, you may want to look into specific tools or techniques designed to minimize external interruptions.
