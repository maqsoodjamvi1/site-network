When you are in the middle of a breakup, your brain feels like a browser with fifty tabs open, and half of them are frozen. You have to mourn the loss of a life you built, but you also have to figure out who is paying the electric bill, how to handle the shared lease, and whether you can manage the presentation due at work on Tuesday.

This isn’t just emotional distress; it is cognitive overload. Decision fatigue is the reality of having a limited supply of willpower. When you spend your morning crying in the shower or spiraling over a text message, you are literally burning the glucose your brain needs to make rational, long-term decisions.

Here is how to stop the bleed and keep your life running while your heart is still catching up.

## The "Low-Stakes" Protocol for Work
When you’re grieving, your work performance often suffers because you are trying to make complex decisions while your executive function is compromised. Stop trying to be 100% productive. Instead, move into "maintenance mode."

If you have a job that allows for any autonomy, identify the "must-haves" and ignore the rest for two weeks. Send the emails that will cause a crisis if ignored, but stop polishing the slide deck that no one is really looking at. If you are struggling to focus, use the "Two-Minute Rule": if a task can be done in two minutes, do it immediately to get it off your plate. If it takes longer, put it on a physical notepad—not your phone—to deal with during a dedicated 30-minute block. Keep that block at the end of your workday when the morning’s emotional intensity has usually waned.

## Offload Your Logistics
You cannot handle breakup logistics and your daily life simultaneously. If you are moving out, don't try to sort through your entire life in one weekend. You will end up keeping things you don't need or throwing away things you’ll regret.

Instead, create a "Triage System" for your possessions and paperwork:
*   **The "Neutral" Box:** Anything that belongs to your ex or is a shared item goes into a box immediately. Seal it, label it, and put it in a closet or trunk. Do not look inside. Getting these items out of your visual field reduces the amount of processing power your brain spends on them every time you walk into a room.
*   **The "Essential" List:** Write down the three things that *must* happen this week (e.g., change the Netflix password, call the landlord, update your direct deposit). Nothing else matters. If it isn't on the list, give yourself permission to defer it until next week.
*   **The Automation Hack:** If you have the budget, outsource the low-level decisions. Order the same meal prep for five days so you don’t have to choose what to eat. Set your bills to auto-pay. Use a laundry service for a week if the thought of folding clothes makes you want to crawl back into bed.

## Stop the "What-If" Loophole
The most exhausting part of a breakup is the mental negotiation. You spend hours replaying conversations, trying to find the point where things went wrong. This is a trap. Your brain is trying to find a solution to a problem that has already been solved.

When you catch yourself in a loop, physically interrupt the pattern. If you’re at your desk, stand up and walk to a different room. If you’re in bed, sit on the floor. Tell yourself: "I am not allowed to analyze the relationship until 6:00 PM." Then, give yourself fifteen minutes of strictly timed reflection. If you have a journal, write it down. Once the timer goes off, force a transition—put on a podcast, call a friend, or start a task that requires physical movement.

## Create a "Safety Default"
Decision fatigue leads to impulsive choices—like drunk-texting your ex or quitting your job out of spite. To prevent this, establish a "Safety Default" for when your emotional tank hits empty. 

Choose a person you trust—a friend or family member—and make them your "checkpoint." Tell them: "I am going through a hard time, and I don't trust my judgment right now. If I’m about to do something major, can I text you first?" You don't even need their advice; you just need to wait for their reply. The 20-minute gap between you feeling the urge and them responding is often enough time for the adrenaline to fade and your logic to return.

## Managing the Social Fallout
You will have to explain the breakup to coworkers, friends, or family. Repeating the story over and over is a major source of fatigue. Create a "scripts" document on your phone. Write out a brief, two-sentence explanation for different groups of people. 

For your boss: "I’m going through a personal transition, so I’m keeping things simple for the next few weeks. I’m still on top of my projects."
For a friend: "I’m not quite ready to talk about the details yet, but I appreciate you checking in."

Having these ready removes the need to improvise while you’re already feeling raw.

Breaking up is a logistical project as much as it is an emotional one. By treating your decision-making capacity like a limited resource, you can preserve your energy for the things that actually matter—like your own long-term health and stability. Focus on the next right step rather than the entire horizon. You don’t need to see the end of the journey; you just need to get through the next few hours. 

As you start to stabilize your daily routine, you may find that the fog begins to lift, allowing you to assess what you truly want for your next chapter. Addressing the immediate chaos is the first step, but as your baseline settles, you might find you need more specific guidance on how to navigate the deeper emotional integration of your new single life.
