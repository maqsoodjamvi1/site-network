If you have ever finished a video call, stared at the wall for three minutes, and realized you have no idea what you are supposed to do next, you aren’t just "distracted." You are suffering from a cognitive hangover.

In the mental health space, we talk a lot about burnout from working too much. But the more immediate, daily culprit is something called "attention residue." When you jump from a budget review meeting to writing a marketing brief, your brain doesn't actually clear the cache. It carries over the stress, the social anxiety, and the unfinished thoughts from the previous task. This leaves you feeling sluggish, irritable, and incapable of focused work.

Over the last few years, I’ve had to stop treating the five minutes between meetings as "dead time" to answer emails. Instead, I started using that time to perform a "cognitive reset." Here is how you can do the same, without needing a dedicated meditation room or an extra hour in your day.

## Stop Trying to "Do One More Thing"

The biggest mistake we make is using the gap between meetings to "get ahead." We think, *I have seven minutes before my next Zoom, I can knock out these three Slack messages.*

This is exactly what creates the hangover. You are forcing your brain to switch contexts instantly. By the time the next meeting starts, you haven't processed the information from the first one, and you haven't settled into the second. You are working at half-capacity.

Instead, commit to a "transition period." If your meeting ends at 10:55, your next task doesn't start until 11:00. Those five minutes belong to your nervous system, not your inbox.

## The Physical "Close-Out" Ritual

Your brain uses physical cues to categorize information. If you do everything in the same chair, facing the same screen, your brain struggles to distinguish between "meeting mode" and "deep work mode."

When a meeting ends, perform a physical ritual to signal that the chapter is closed. You don't need anything elaborate. Try these:

*   **The Screen Flip:** If you use a laptop, physically close it, even if just for two minutes. If you use a desktop, minimize every window until you are looking at your desktop wallpaper.
*   **The Change of Perspective:** Stand up and move to a different part of the room. If you are in a home office, go to the kitchen to get a glass of water. If you are in a cubicle, walk to the water cooler or just stand and look out a window.
*   **The Sensory Reset:** Splash cold water on your wrists or face. The drop in temperature is a proven way to jolt your nervous system out of a loop of anxious, work-related thinking.

These aren't just "breaks." They are boundaries. They tell your brain, *That topic is finished. We are moving on.*

## The "Brain Dump" as a Bridge

Sometimes, the reason we can’t switch tasks is that we are worried we will forget the "to-dos" that came up during the meeting. We stay in a state of low-level alarm because we are afraid of dropping a ball.

Keep a physical notepad—not a digital one—next to your keyboard. The moment the meeting ends, spend 60 seconds writing down the three most important things you need to do next, or any lingering questions that are bothering you. 

Once they are on paper, your brain no longer has to expend energy "holding" those items in your active memory. You have successfully offloaded the cognitive load. You can now close your eyes, take three deep breaths, and let the previous meeting go.

## Managing the "Post-Meeting Slump"

Some meetings are naturally more draining than others. If you just came out of a performance review or a difficult project update, your body is likely holding onto cortisol. You might feel a tightness in your chest or a clenched jaw.

In these cases, a simple "reset" isn't enough. You need to discharge the energy. Try the "5-4-3-2-1" grounding technique, but shorten it for your workspace:

*   **Acknowledge 5 things you can see:** (e.g., your coffee mug, a plant, your keyboard, a light switch, your phone).
*   **Acknowledge 4 things you can feel:** (e.g., your feet on the floor, your back against the chair, the weight of your watch, the fabric of your sleeves).
*   **Take 3 deep, slow breaths.**

This forces your brain to re-engage with your physical environment, which is the fastest way to stop an "overthinking" spiral after a high-pressure conversation.

## Reclaiming Your Mental Real Estate

If you find yourself constantly drained by the end of the day, it is likely because your brain is running a thousand background processes from meetings that happened hours ago. You aren't tired because you worked too hard; you are tired because you never allowed your brain to fully "unload" the previous task before starting the next one.

Building these small, non-negotiable gaps into your schedule is not a luxury. It is a necessary maintenance step for your focus and your mental health. By treating your brain like a computer that needs to clear its cache rather than a machine that should run indefinitely, you’ll find that the "hangover" effect begins to fade.

Start small. Pick one of these tactics to try after your very next meeting today. Notice how your body feels when you allow yourself those few minutes of transition, and observe how much more present you feel when the next calendar notification pings.
