We often think intimacy is built during grand gestures—a weekend getaway, a deep late-night talk, or a milestone anniversary. In reality, the health of a long-term relationship is determined by the dozens of tiny, almost invisible exchanges that happen while you’re making coffee or folding laundry.

In relationship psychology, these moments are called "bids for connection." A bid is any attempt from one partner to another for attention, affirmation, affection, or any other positive connection. It can be as simple as pointing out a bird in the yard, sighing about a stressful email, or asking, "Did you see where I put my keys?"

How you respond to these bids—either by turning toward them, turning against them, or ignoring them—is the primary predictor of whether your relationship will grow or drift apart over time.

## What a "Bid" Actually Looks Like

Most people miss bids because they don’t look like requests for intimacy. They look like noise. Here is how they typically show up in your living room:

*   **The Observation:** You say, "Wow, that construction project down the street is finally finished." You aren’t really talking about road work; you’re saying, "I’m here, I’m noticing things, and I want to share my reality with you."
*   **The Emotional Trial Balloon:** Your partner says, "I am so tired of this project at work." They aren’t necessarily asking for a solution or a lecture on time management. They are checking to see if you are a safe harbor for their frustration.
*   **The Physical Reach:** A hand placed on your shoulder while you’re standing at the kitchen counter, or leaning in to show you a meme on a phone screen.

## The Three Ways You Respond

When a bid happens, your brain makes a split-second choice. You choose to turn toward, turn away, or turn against.

**Turning toward** means acknowledging the bid. You don't have to drop everything you’re doing to have a deep conversation. If your partner points at a bird, you can simply glance over and say, "Oh, yeah, that’s a pretty one," or "I haven't seen one of those in a while." You have validated their reality.

**Turning away** happens when you are distracted. You’re scrolling through your phone, watching TV, or thinking about your to-do list, and you either don't hear them or provide a non-committal grunt. You are physically present, but emotionally absent.

**Turning against** is the most damaging. This is when you respond with hostility or sarcasm. If your partner says, "Hey, look at that bird," and you snap, "Why are you bothering me with that when I’m trying to finish this email?" you aren’t just ignoring the bid; you are actively rejecting the connection.

## How to Practice "Turning Toward" Today

You don't need to change your personality to become better at this. You just need to increase your awareness of the "background noise" in your relationship. Try these three specific steps this week:

*   **The Two-Second Rule:** When your partner speaks to you, even if you are mid-task, take two seconds to physically turn your head or body toward them. That physical pivot signals that their presence matters more than your current distraction.
*   **Identify the "Low-Stakes" Bid:** Start noticing the neutral comments. If your partner mentions the weather or something they heard on the radio, treat it as a deliberate reach for interaction. Respond with a follow-up question instead of a one-word answer.
*   **The "Pause and Validate" Check:** If your partner is venting, stop trying to fix the problem for five minutes. Just mirror their sentiment. If they say, "This meeting was a disaster," say, "That sounds incredibly draining, I’m sorry you had to deal with that." That is a high-level bid-response.

## Building a "Bank Account" of Goodwill

Think of every time you turn toward a bid as a small deposit into an emotional bank account. When life gets stressful—when you have a fight about money or a conflict with in-laws—you need those reserves of goodwill to keep the relationship stable. 

Couples who constantly ignore bids are like people living paycheck to paycheck; they have no emotional savings to fall back on when a crisis hits. By turning toward these tiny moments, you are effectively "pre-paying" for the harder times. You are signaling to your partner, hundreds of times a day, that they are seen, heard, and valued.

Intimacy isn't a destination you reach after a series of big conversations. It is a byproduct of being responsive to the little things. If you want to increase the closeness in your home, start by looking up from your phone the next time your partner says something trivial. You might be surprised at how quickly the atmosphere in the room changes.
